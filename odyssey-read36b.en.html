The Odyssey

Homer · c. 8th century BCE

中文 →

In one sentence

If the Iliad is about how a man chooses to die — for honor, for undying fame, brief and blazing — the Odyssey is about how a man fights his way home to live: refusing a goddess's offer of immortality, enduring ten years of shipwreck and humiliation, all to get back to one bed, one wife, one rocky island. It quietly swaps the definition of the hero from "who can kill best" to "who can endure most, outthink everyone, and never let go of home" — and in doing so becomes the master template for every leave-home, face-trials, return-changed story ever told.

Coordinates

The second of Homer's two epics, born of the same oral tradition as the Iliad (the question of whether "Homer" was one person is taken up at the end). The Iliad is the high noon of war; the Odyssey is its long dusk. Troy has fallen; the other heroes are dead or home; only the many-minded Odysseus is held on the sea road by gods and storms for a full ten years. If the keyword of the Iliad is "rage," the keyword of the Odyssey is "homecoming." (This is the mirror-sibling of READ 36a, the Iliad; together they make the whole of Homer.)

Core claims

The whole poem turns on three things:

  • The highest value is not glory but getting home. Odysseus turns down the goddess Calypso's gift of eternal youth and immortality to return to a mortal wife who will age and die. Through one man's choice, Homer says: human wholeness is finite, rooted in a specific house and specific people — not in never dying.
  • What carries you home alive is not force but cunning. Achilles fought with his fists; Odysseus wins with his mind. This epic moves the hero's defining virtue from courage to intelligence — the wit to disguise, to lie, to wait, to endure.
  • The floor of civilization is how you treat a stranger. The poem measures every stop with a single ruler — guest-friendship. Those who welcome strangers prosper; those who eat their guests or squat in another man's house are destroyed. Homecoming ends by settling accounts with the men who trampled that law, and rebuilding order.

The core ideas, one by one

Nostos (homecoming): why he refuses immortality to go home

Nostos is Greek for "return home" — and the root of our word nostalgia (literally "the pain of homecoming"). It is the engine of the whole poem: every adventure is only a detour forced upon the road home. The idea is nailed down hardest in the Calypso episode. The goddess loves him, keeps him on her island for seven years, and offers him the ultimate bargain: deathlessness, unfading youth, and herself as wife — the very price any mortal is supposed to dream of. Yet Odysseus sits on the shore and weeps, looking toward home. He tells her, in what is almost the poem's thesis (paraphrased): I know my wife Penelope cannot match you — she is mortal and will grow old; you are a goddess and never fade — and still, all my days and nights, I long only for home.

Why does this matter? Because it is the first time Western literature says so plainly: what makes us human, and whole, is precisely that we are finite. Immortality means standing still, stepping outside time, leaving behind the specific, aging, losable home — and human meaning grows exactly in that liability to loss. Homer makes the shrewdest, most survival-hungry of heroes push the deathless door shut of his own will. It changes how you weigh things: next time you can't tell "more" from "better" — a longer life, a bigger house, a safer somewhere-else — remember that Odysseus chose an island that would grow old over a paradise that never would.

Mētis and the "man of many turns" (polytropos): the mind as weapon

Mētis is a very particular kind of Greek intelligence — not book-smarts or logic, but adaptive, improvising, force-beating practical cunning: the wit of the carpenter sizing timber, the helmsman reading wind, the hunter setting a snare, the diplomat working a room. Odysseus is mētis made flesh. The very first word used to describe him in the poem is polytropos — literally "of many turns," meaning both that he was driven all over the world, turned this way and that by fate, and that he is a man of many wiles who can turn a new face at any moment. The pun is the man.

The finest display of mētis is the Cyclops, Polyphemus. The giant traps Odysseus's crew in his cave and eats two men at a sitting. Fight him? He is impossibly strong, and only he can shift the boulder blocking the exit — kill him, and everyone is sealed in the cave to die. Odysseus's whole solution is in his head: first he gets the giant drunk, then gives a false name — "Nobody" (Outis). When he drives a red-hot stake into the giant's single eye and Polyphemus roars for help, the neighbors call through the rock, "Who is hurting you?" — and the giant can only bellow, "Nobody is hurting me!" The neighbors shrug and leave. At dawn, as the blinded giant lets his flock out and gropes each sheep's back for hiding men, Odysseus has his crew cling beneath the sheep's bellies and slip free. Weak beats strong, wit beats muscle — this scene is the ancestor of a thousand later "outsmart the monster" set pieces.

But Homer refuses to make mētis simply admirable. Safely away, Odysseus cannot resist shouting his real name back at the giant to gloat — and that one lapse gives Polyphemus the name to curse him to his father, the sea-god Poseidon, unleashing the ten years of disaster to come. Cunning carries its own opposite inside it: the pride that cannot keep its mouth shut. Homer's point: cleverness is the price of survival, but what the clever man can least defend against is his own hunger to be recognized and praised.

Xenia (guest-friendship): the ruler that measures civilization

Xenia is the sacred host-guest code of the ancient Greek world: a stranger arrives, and the host must first feed him, bathe him, give him a bed, give him gifts, and see him safely on his way — and only then ask who he is and where he's from. This is not politeness; it is law underwritten by Zeus himself (one of his titles is "protector of guests"). In a world with no inns, no police, and danger the moment you leave your door, welcoming the stranger is the only glue civilization has. The Odyssey is almost a running audit of xenia — Homer uses it to grade every stop and every person.

The negative pole is the Cyclops: Odysseus comes asking for exactly this guest-right, and the giant answers, "We Cyclopes care nothing for your gods," then eats his guests for a snack — his blinding is earned. The positive pole is the Phaeacians, who lavish hospitality on the shipwrecked Odysseus and promise to sail him home before they've even learned his name — they win the good name Homer keeps praising. And the great collapse of xenia is in Odysseus's own house: over a hundred suitors squat in his palace, eating his food, drinking his wine, pressuring his wife to remarry, and plotting to kill his son — a total trampling of the guest-law. So the climax of homecoming is not only "he gets home" but "he settles accounts with these rotten guests": Odysseus must re-erect, with his own hands, the household rule they have wrecked. Understand xenia and you finally see why the bloody slaughter at the end reads, in Homer's frame, as justice rather than cruelty — it is the forced repair of civilization's floor.

Disguise, testing, and recognition (anagnorisis): identity redeemed one relic at a time

Back on Ithaca, Odysseus does not stride in and announce, "I'm home." Athena turns him into a ragged old beggar, and in that shape he slips into his own palace to watch, cold-eyed, who is loyal and who is treacherous, who deserves to live and who to die. The whole back half of the poem runs on a series of recognitions — anagnorisis, Greek for "the passage from not-knowing to knowing," which Aristotle in the Poetics names the key mechanism of drama: who can recognize that this beggar is the king? Never by announcement, but by one intimate old object, one shared secret, at a time.

The old dog Argos — the hunting hound Odysseus raised before he sailed, now aged and diseased on a dung heap — is the first in the house to know him: he catches the scent, lifts his head, wags his tail once, and dies. The old nurse washing the beggar's feet runs her hand over the scar a boar's tusk left on his leg in boyhood, and startles so hard she drops his foot. And the most moving recognition is between husband and wife: Penelope, refusing to trust too easily, orders a servant to "move the master's bed out of the bedroom for our guest" — and Odysseus flares up, because he built that bed himself, using a living olive tree as one bedpost and raising the whole chamber around it, rooted in the earth, immovable by anyone. He blurts out the secret of the bed, and only then does Penelope fall into his arms. Homer plants a perfect metaphor here: their marriage, like that bed, is rooted in the ground — twenty years, and it cannot be moved. Identity is never proven by saying "I am I"; it is redeemed, piece by piece, by a scar, a dog, a bed, a secret only two people share.

Penelope: her cunning matches his

The Odyssey is not a one-man show. His wife Penelope holds the household alone for twenty years with the same weapon her husband carries — mētis. Against a hundred-plus suitors demanding she remarry, she agrees to choose "as soon as I finish weaving this shroud for my father-in-law," then weaves by day and secretly unravels it by night, so a single cloth stays unfinished for three years, stalling the marriage — until a servant betrays the trick. This "web of Penelope" is cut from the same cloth as Odysseus's "Nobody." And in the end it is she who tests him, using the immovable bed to flush out and verify the real man. Homer makes the couple equals in guile — striking in an ancient tradition that so often writes women as trophies or temptresses: their reunion moves us precisely because it is two equally clever, equally patient, well-matched people recognizing each other across twenty years.

Temptation and forgetting: every stop urges him "just stay"

String Odysseus's sea adventures together and they turn out to be not random spectacle but a series of tests of homecoming — nearly every stop tempts him, in some form, to stop, forget, and stop trying to get home:

  • The Lotus-eaters: eat the fruit and you forget your homeland and only want to stay — the temptation to erase memory with ease; Odysseus has to drag his men back to the ship by force.
  • The witch Circe: she turns men into pigs (a metaphor for losing human form to appetite), yet Odysseus lingers a year in her bed, nearly forgetting to leave.
  • The Sirens: their song lures sailors onto the rocks to die — and Homer reveals what they actually promise is total knowledge. Wanting to hear it without dying, Odysseus has his crew plug their ears with wax and lash him to the mast: he experiences the temptation without being destroyed by it.
  • The goddess Calypso: as above, holding him seven years with immortality itself.

Lined up, the theme surfaces: the great enemy of getting home is rarely storm or monster — it is the comfort and forgetting of "staying here wouldn't be so bad." To be human (rather than a pig, a zombie, or a deathless god) is to refuse, again and again, the sweet exits that would make you forget who you are and where you were going. In the twentieth century, the philosophers Adorno and Horkheimer even read the Siren episode as the birth of the modern rational self: Odysseus binding himself, trading self-restraint for safe passage, is the prototype of "civilized man buying self-preservation at the cost of repressing desire" (a powerful modern reading — see below).

Structure and narrative: how the epic tells itself "from the middle"

As a piece of storytelling craft, the Odyssey is a landmark, in two especially influential ways. First is beginning in the middle of the action (Latin in medias res, "into the midst of things"): the poem does not start from the end of the Trojan War but plunges straight into year ten, with Odysseus stranded on Calypso's island; the most famous adventures at sea are told later, in the hero's own flashback, at the Phaeacians' feast. Which means the Cyclops, the Sirens, the trip to the underworld are "a story the protagonist tells to others" — a not-fully-reliable narrator, showing off. Second is its double plot: the first four books contain no Odysseus at all, following instead his son Telemachus as he leaves home in search of his father and grows from a timid boy into a man who can act (scholars call these books the "Telemachy"). The father seeks home on the sea; the son seeks his father — and himself — on land; the two threads finally converge on Ithaca to clear out the suitors together. Three thousand years ago, Homer had already mastered flashback, parallel plotting, and the unreliable narrator — the whole toolkit of the modern novel.

The essential skeleton

Compress this heavy epic to a single line: its argument is that human wholeness lies not in immortality or glory but in returning to the finite, specific home — and that you reach it not by force but by cunning and endurance.

How does it prove this? Through a carefully built structure of contrast. First it sets the price: a goddess offers immortality, and Odysseus chooses a mortal home — from the opening, the finite home is ranked above the infinite deathless one. Then a chain of adventures applies pressure: Lotus-eaters, Circe, Sirens, Calypso, each a temptation to "just stay," and each time he chooses to keep going — the will to return, tempered by temptation, becomes believable. Meanwhile a second thread grows the son and lets order at home decay (the suitors trampling xenia), stacking the reasons "he must go back" ever higher: home is not merely something he wants but something only he can save. Then a triple close: recognition (identity redeemed relic by relic), reckoning (the great bow strung, the arrow through twelve axes, the suitors slaughtered), and reunion (the immovable marriage-bed proving the true spouses). So "homecoming" pays out on three levels at once in the ending — the home of place, the home of order, and the home of a person. A wild-seeming adventure tale rests on a tightly reasoned proof: that getting home is worth pushing away immortality, enduring ten years, and cutting a bloody path to reach it.

Common misreadings & honest criticism

Misreading 1: it's just a great adventure/travel story. The Cyclops, the Sirens, the witch are the memorable set pieces, but they occupy only four of the poem's twenty-four books (nine through twelve) — and they are told in flashback. The real center of gravity is the second half: the disguise, testing, recognition, and restoration back on Ithaca. Read it as thrill-seeking spectacle and you miss exactly what Homer cares about most — getting home, and, once home, re-proving "who I am and that this place is mine."

Misreading 2: Odysseus is a spotless hero. He is a "sacker of cities" and does plenty of raiding on the way; he lies constantly (he even feeds his own father a false tale on his return); and at the end he not only kills every suitor but orders the hanging of twelve slave women who had slept with them — a scene whose coldness unsettles many modern readers. The contemporary novelist Margaret Atwood retold the whole story from the viewpoint of those slaves and of Penelope (The Penelopiad), forcing the question: from someone else's angle, is that "resourceful" hero a lying, violent master? Homer's Odysseus is never a moral model but a complex, shadowed "man of many sides" — which is exactly what makes him feel more modern than Achilles.

Dispute 1: who was "Homer," really? (the Homeric Question) Scholars of the 19th and 20th centuries — above all Milman Parry and Albert Lord — concluded that these epics were not written at a desk by a single genius but are the crystallization of centuries of oral tradition: bards improvised in performance from a stock of ready-made "formulas" (the recurring tags like "rosy-fingered Dawn" and "resourceful Odysseus"), polished across generations. "Homer" may not be one man but the name of that whole tradition. This explains why the epics are so full of repeated formulas, and trades the romance of "the genius author" for the colder truth of "collective oral composition" — though the argument is far from settled.

Dispute 2: is a poem about restoring the master to his home fair to women and slaves? As above, the hanging of the slave women, and the writing of Penelope's fidelity as a piece of her husband's property, disturb modern readers. Homer's defenders answer that we can't try a world three thousand years old by today's morality — within the poem's ethical frame that slaughter is "repairing an order that had been trampled" — and that Penelope herself is written with real intelligence and agency, no passive ornament. Both readings hold; and a classic is a classic precisely because it can bear, and reward, being questioned this way again and again.
