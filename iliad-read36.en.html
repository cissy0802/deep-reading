The Iliad

Homer · c. 8th century BCE

中文 →

In one sentence

This is not a history of the Trojan War. It is the story of one man's rage over a single humiliation, in the last few weeks of a ten-year war — how that rage kills the person he loves most, and how it finally goes quiet in the tears of his enemy's father. It is the first work of Western literature: all spears and glory on the surface, and underneath, the deepest pity ever written for the fact that human beings die.

Coordinates

Whether "Homer" was a real person, one man or the collective signature of a singing tradition, scholars still cannot say (see "the Homeric Question" below). What is certain: this poem of roughly fifteen thousand lines is where the Greek oral-epic tradition crystallized, around the 8th century BCE, in a solemn meter called dactylic hexameter. Together with its sister poem, the Odyssey, it stands at the headwaters of all Western literature — tragedy, epic, and the very idea of "the hero" grow out of it. Don't let the word "epic" scare you off: what it is actually about is startlingly modern — being disrespected, sulking, losing a friend, taking revenge, and a man learning to make peace with his own death.

Core claims

Everything turns on three things — and the very first word of the poem throws the theme in your face: "wrath" (μῆνις, mēnis):

  • A war epic whose real protagonist is an emotion. The Iliad skips the war's cause (the abduction of Helen) and its end (the Trojan Horse). It fixes only on the wrath of Achilles: how it is lit, where it turns, how it destroys everything, and how it is finally dissolved.
  • The heroic honor code is both magnificent and self-destroying. What drives these men to die is kleos — undying fame — trading a short life for a name that outlasts it. That code makes men great, and it also drags a whole army into death over who gets to keep one war-prize.
  • It is precisely because they die that mortals matter. The gods here are all-powerful yet shallow, because they have nothing at stake; humans are small, they hurt, they die — and it is death that gives every one of their choices weight and beauty.

The core concepts, one at a time

Wrath (μῆνις, mēnis): the whole poem is built around one man's anger

The Iliad does not open with "long ago." Its first word is mēnis — "Sing, goddess, the wrath." In Greek the word is heavy; it is normally reserved for the anger of a god, and Homer lays it on a mortal. The structure of the entire poem is the arc of that anger: first it is aimed at his own side (Agamemnon, commander of the Greek host) and gets countless Greek comrades killed; then, when his beloved friend Patroclus dies, it wheels around toward the Trojan champion Hector and burns into a frenzy of desecrating the corpse; and finally, before Hector's aged father, it softens at last.

The point is not "he got angry" but that Homer rests the weight of an entire epic on a single private emotion: not fate, not the will of the gods, not the honor of nations — one man who cannot swallow an insult. Three thousand years ago this was a thunderclap of a move: it announced that literature's most durable subject is not "what happened" but how a single heart bears what happens to it. Grasp that, and you understand why it sits first in the Western canon.

Honor (τιμή, timē) and the prize (γέρας, geras): what the quarrel is really about

The story's fuse is a dispute over the division of spoils — but if you read it as "fighting over a woman," you miss the poem's whole economics. Timē means "honor," but in Homer's world it is not an abstract reputation; it is something weighable, something that can be taken from you — how much loot you are allotted is exactly how much timē you are worth. The particular prize that marks status is called geras, the prize of honor.

Here is what happens: Agamemnon is forced to give back a female captive of his own (Apollo has sent a plague over her), cannot stomach the loss, and seizes Achilles' captive Briseis as compensation. Note carefully — Achilles rages not out of love but because he has been publicly stripped of his geras: in a society that prices honor openly in war-prizes, taking his geras is a declaration, in front of the whole army, that "you are worth less than you thought." This is a zero-sum honor game: honor is finite; more for me means less for you; so an insult must be answered in blood. Once you see that timē is a visible currency of rank, you understand why these towering heroes will turn on each other over a captive to the point of letting their allies die — what they are defending is not the woman but their own valuation among men.

Undying fame (κλέος, kleos) and two fates: the choice of Achilles

Kleos literally means "that which is heard" — reputation, glory, the thing passed from mouth to mouth. For the Homeric hero, a man must die and the body cannot be kept; the only immortality is that later generations keep singing your name (the poets call it kleos aphthiton, imperishable fame). This is the engine of the entire warrior ethic: they charge into death to win a name that will not die.

Achilles lays the ledger out most nakedly. In Book 9, when an embassy comes with lavish gifts to beg him to fight, he reveals his secret — his goddess mother has told him of two fates (moira): stay home and live long in obscurity, his name dying with his body; or stay at Troy, fall in battle, and live forever in song. At first, in his fury, he leans toward the first (going home) — which is itself a tremor in the whole heroic logic. But after Patroclus dies, knowing there is no turning back (his mother has foretold: once Hector falls, your own death follows close behind), he goes out to fight anyway. He knowingly trades an entire peaceful lifetime for an undying name — and that self-awareness turns him from a brute into the first tragic hero of the West. He does not misunderstand the cost; he understands it and chooses.

Patroclus: the man who makes the wrath change direction

The hinge of the whole poem is the profound bond between Achilles and his dearest companion, Patroclus. While Achilles refuses to fight, Patroclus cannot bear to watch their comrades fall one after another, and begs him in tears: if you will not go, at least lend me your armor, and let me wear it to frighten the Trojans off. Achilles agrees. Patroclus fights in the borrowed armor, drives the enemy back to the walls — but, blood up and overstepping Achilles' warning, he is killed by Hector (with Apollo's help).

When the news comes, Achilles' reaction shakes the earth: he smears dirt over his head and face, rolls on the ground, tears his hair, refuses food and drink, and wants only a quick death and revenge. Look closely at what powers this turn: what sends Achilles back to battle and to a willing death is not honor, not gifts, not strategy, but grief for a lost friend. The same man whom all Agamemnon's gold could not move now throws his life away for a dead friend. His wrath undergoes a change of kind here — from the wounded pride of "someone took my honor" it is transfigured into the towering grief of "I have lost the one I love" — something deeper, more human, and more deadly. (Ancient readers such as Aeschylus and Plato's Symposium read the two as lovers; Homer's own text writes only a bond of the deepest kind, and whether it is erotic love has always been open ground for interpretation.)

Fate (μοῖρα, moira) and the gods: even Zeus cannot save his own son

Moira translates literally as "a portion, a share" — the allotment of life and death assigned to you is fixed. In Homer's world there is a subtle hierarchy: fate stands above even the gods. The most piercing scene is in Book 16: Zeus's mortal son Sarpedon is fated to die at Patroclus' hands, and the king of the gods, moved to pity, wants to save him — but his wife Hera coldly reminds him that if he breaks the law of fate, the other gods will rush to save their own children too, and the whole order collapses. Zeus weeps tears of blood, and can only watch his son die. Even the highest god cannot alter fate; he can only mourn — the poem's coldest note on the limits of power.

And the gods themselves are, in Homer's hands, an uncomfortable crowd: they split into factions (some for the Greeks, some for Troy), they squabble and cheat over mortal wars, and they never pay a price — because they cannot die. Their "combat" is play; the mortals' combat is blood in every line. That stark contrast is exactly Homer's design: the gods have everything yet are trivial, because they have nothing at stake; humans have nothing to lean on yet are dignified, because what they wager is the one life they get.

Mortality (θνητός, thnētos): generations like leaves

This is the water table beneath the whole epic. Again and again Homer reminds you that these heroes are "mortals" (thnētoi). Book 6 holds a famous image — the Trojan warrior Glaucus, asked on the battlefield about his lineage, answers (paraphrased): "The generations of men are like leaves. The wind scatters the old leaves to the ground, but the tree in spring buds new ones; so one generation of men grows even as another passes away." To speak of men as falling leaves at the very moment one should be playing the hero — that is Homer's ground color.

This is why the most moving thing in the Iliad is often not the killing but the Homeric simile: in the middle of a slaughter, Homer will suddenly cut to the world of peace — a warrior falls "as a poplar is felled by a craftsman"; an army swarms "like autumn flies around the milk pails"; a mother waving a fly from her sleeping child. These images, borrowed from farming, herding, and home, are so many small windows, each reminding you that behind every name about to be run through by a spear is a whole life that could have farmed, loved, and raised children. Homer writes war, and spends everything to make you unwilling to lose a single one of the dead — that is the secret of a poem that writes war yet works against it.

The Shield of Achilles (Book 18): the whole world engraved on one shield

After Patroclus falls, Achilles' old armor is stripped by Hector, and his mother Thetis begs the smith-god Hephaestus to forge new armor overnight. Here Homer halts the fighting and spends more than a hundred lines describing that shield — a set-piece with its own technical name, ekphrasis (a detailed verbal description of a work of art). What is engraved on the shield is not weapons but the whole of human life: earth, sky, sea, sun, moon, and stars; two cities — one at peace (a wedding, dancing, a lawsuit argued in the square), one at war (a siege, an ambush); and plowing, harvest, a vineyard, grazing herds, a dancing floor of young men and women; and around the outermost rim, the great river Ocean encircling the world.

Why insert such an encyclopedic panorama of peace at the bloodiest moment? Because that is precisely everything that lies outside war and everything war destroys: marriages, harvests, dancing, ordinary days. Achilles is about to carry this shield out to kill and to die, and yet the shield is covered with the teeming ordinary life he is about to leave forever. The shield is the poem's theme in miniature: it tells you, without a word, what this slaughter truly puts at stake — a whole, warm, living human world.

Pity (Book 24): Priam and Achilles, and how the wrath goes out

The emotional summit of the poem is not any victory but its ending. To avenge Patroclus, Achilles kills Hector and then, day after day, drags his corpse behind his chariot around the walls — the wrath has curdled into desecration of the dead. At that point the old Trojan king, Hector's father Priam, crosses into the enemy camp alone under cover of night, comes into the tent of the man who killed his son, and does something unthinkable: he kneels and kisses the very hands that "killed so many of my sons," begging only to ransom the body for burial.

Achilles could rage again, but as he looks at this old man, as aged as his own father, he suddenly thinks of his father far away — and the two enemies weep together, one for a dead son, the other for the son he too will lose, the son fated to die young. In that moment the killer and the bereaved recognize each other in the one fact that all people die and lose what they love, and the wrath finally dissolves. Achilles returns the body and grants a truce so the Trojans can bury Hector. The poem ends on Hector's funeral — no wooden horse, no fallen city, no triumph; it chooses to stop on grief and one fragile ceasefire. That restraint is the Iliad's greatest stroke: it tells you war has no true winners, and that the highest comfort available to human beings is a single instant of mutual recognition between enemies.

The essential skeleton

Compress fifteen thousand lines into one line, and it is "the birth and death of a rage":

  • Ignition: Agamemnon seizes Achilles' prize of honor (Briseis), breaking his timē in public.
  • Withdrawal: Achilles quits the fighting and asks his mother to have Zeus make the Greeks lose, so the whole army will taste the cost of "without me."
  • The price: the Greeks are driven back, corpses everywhere. An embassy comes with rich gifts (Book 9); Achilles refuses — right now he would rather keep his life than win a name.
  • The turn: his friend Patroclus fights in Achilles' armor, turns the tide for a while, and is killed by Hector.
  • The wrath wheels: Achilles' rage swings from Agamemnon entirely onto Hector — and onto fate itself. He returns to battle knowing it means choosing his own death.
  • The frenzy: he kills Hector and defiles the corpse; the wrath spills past the bounds of the human.
  • The going-out: Priam's tears make him recognize their shared mortality; he returns the body. The poem ends on Hector's funeral.

What does it prove, and how? — It proves that the logic of honor pushed to its limit is self-destruction, and that the only thing able to check that destroying force is not a greater honor but a shared acknowledgment that "we all die." It proves this not by preaching but by letting you watch the honor code devour man after man, and then, in the final book, using the tears of two enemies to make the whole logic come loose on the spot.

Common misreadings & honest criticism

Misreading one: it tells the whole Trojan War. No. It covers only about fifty days in the tenth year. The golden apple and Paris stealing Helen are the war's pretext and lie outside the poem; the wooden horse and the fall of Troy come after it; even Achilles' own death (the arrow to the heel) is only foretold, never staged — those belong to the Odyssey and the later Epic Cycle, not to the Iliad. It is a poem about a few weeks in a war, not a chronicle of the war.

Misreading two: it glorifies war and force. The opposite. In her famous essay "The Iliad, or the Poem of Force" (1939), the philosopher Simone Weil put it exactly: the poem's true protagonist is force (la force) — the kind of violence that turns a human into a thing: it turns the one it strikes into a corpse (a thing entirely), and it also swells, blinds, and finally destroys the one who wields it. Homer pities both sides equally: he never lets you cheer for "our side" and jeer at "the enemy"; he makes you grieve for every person the force rolls over. It is a poem that lays out war's glory and its cost at once, honest to the edge of anti-war.

The Homeric Question: was there really a Homer? This is the great scholarly dispute. In the 20th century, Milman Parry and Albert Lord, studying living oral singers in what was then Yugoslavia, proposed the theory of oral-formulaic composition: the Iliad was not written at a desk by one genius but is the accumulated, improvised crystallization of an oral tradition over generations. The evidence is in the text's repeated "formulas" — "swift-footed Achilles," "the wine-dark sea," "rosy-fingered dawn" — the scaffolding a singer used to fill the meter and keep the song flowing. So "Homer" may not be a man but the name of a whole tradition. This does not diminish the poem; it makes it more astonishing: a work polished by centuries of collective oral telling nonetheless holds a tragic vision this unified and this deep.

The moral problem of the gods. Many readers, ancient and modern (Plato among them), are troubled by Homer's frivolous, jealous gods — they lie, take sides, use mortals as pieces. But that is precisely the point: Homer's gods are not moral models but the mirror-image of "those with nothing at stake." Their shallowness throws the dignity of mortals into relief. Make the gods perfect, and human death stops being moving.

The essence in ten lines

  • 1. The poem's first word is "wrath" — it skips the war's cause and its end and tracks only how one man's un-swallowable insult destroys everything and then goes quiet.
  • 2. The quarrel looks like a fight over a captive but is really about honor (timē): in a world that prices rank openly in war-prizes, taking your prize declares to everyone that you are worth less.
  • 3. Honor is zero-sum — more for me means less for you — so an insult must be paid in blood; that logic, taken to its limit, is self-destruction.
  • 4. What drives the hero to die is undying fame (kleos): the body rots, and only the name sung by later generations does not die.
  • 5. Achilles' greatness is that he sees the two fates clearly — safe and nameless, or short-lived and immortal — and chooses the second with open eyes; he knows the cost and chooses anyway.
  • 6. Fate stands above the gods: even Zeus cannot save the son who is fated to die, and can only weep tears of blood — the coldest note on the limits of power.
  • 7. The gods are all-powerful yet trivial, because they have nothing at stake; mortals have nothing to lean on yet are dignified, because they wager the one life they get.
  • 8. "The generations of men are like leaves, one grows as another falls" — Homer, on the very battlefield where one should play the hero, keeps reminding you that each of the dead had a whole life.
  • 9. The poem's highest moment is no victory but the killer Achilles and the bereaved king Priam weeping together, recognizing each other in the fact that all people die and lose what they love.
  • 10. It ends on a funeral, not a triumph: war has no winners, and the highest comfort available is one instant of mutual recognition between enemies — the fire of honor must be put out by pity.
