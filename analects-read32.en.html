The Analects

Lúnyǔ · Confucius (Kǒngzǐ) · compiled c. 5th century BCE by his disciples

中文 →

In One Sentence

How do you become a good person—and, through that, make the world between people go well? Confucius's answer runs through neither God, nor the coercive force of law, nor some grand abstract doctrine, but through the single project of cultivating yourself: first growing rén inside you (a genuine care for others), then using lǐ (ritual, form) to give it exact shape in your daily conduct, and then letting it ripple outward from self to family to state, like rings spreading on water. This is not a tidy system of philosophy but the recorded conversations of a teacher and his students—yet from these scattered, endlessly chewable exchanges grew the default script by which the Chinese world, for two thousand years, answered the questions how should a person behave, treat others, and govern?

Coordinates

Confucius (551–479 BCE)—Kǒng Qiū, styled Zhòngní—lived in the state of Lu at the end of China's Spring and Autumn period. What he most wanted was to hold office and put his Way into practice, and at that he failed everywhere: fourteen years wandering from court to court, and not one ruler would truly employ him. So he withdrew into teaching, took in a reported three thousand students, and pried open learning—once the privilege of aristocrats—to commoners ("in teaching, no distinctions of class"). He himself "transmitted but did not create"—he left not a single written work. What we read as The Analects was compiled after his death, over decades, by his disciples and their disciples: a record of sayings (brief exchanges and aphorisms, deliberately unsystematic), the title Lúnyǔ meaning "edited conversations." Its place is irreplaceable: this is the founding classic of Confucianism, and after Emperor Wu of Han "dismissed the hundred schools and honored Confucianism alone," it became for two millennia the core curriculum of the scholar-official, the heart of the imperial examinations, and the bedrock of state ideology. To understand the deep temperament of Chinese society—why it weighs human relationships, family, face, and "being a good person" above believing in any god—you cannot go around this book. Reading it is like reading Socrates: the man wrote nothing; it all survives because his students wrote it down.

The Core Claims

Five hundred–odd exchanges, but they bite down on three things:

  • The root of being human is rén—a genuine, heartfelt care for others; without it every virtue and every rule is an empty shell. "If a person is not rén, what has he to do with ritual?"—strip out the care, and the forms are hollow theater.
  • You do not become good by birth or by sudden enlightenment, but through a lifetime of learning and practice—doing the right thing over and over until it is worked into your character. Confucius never dwells on cosmic mysteries; he talks only about what is close at hand and can be started today: filial love, trustworthiness, loyalty, reciprocity, self-discipline.
  • The logic of governing is the same as the logic of self-cultivation: the ruler first sets himself right, and his virtue draws and transforms people like a magnetic field—rather than pressing them down with punishment. "If he is upright himself, things get done without orders; if he is not upright, though he order, none obey."

The Core Concepts, One by One

Rén (benevolence): the book's beating heart—which it pointedly refuses to define

Rén is the central word of The Analects, appearing over a hundred times; the character is written as "two people"—it is never about a lone individual perfecting himself in isolation, but about the genuine warmth that should exist between people. Asked flatly by his student Fan Chi what rén is, Confucius answers in two words: "to love others." But you notice something strange: nearly every student who asks about rén gets a different answer—the over-reacher is told "master yourself and return to ritual," the chatterbox is told "the person of rén is cautious in speech," the aspiring official is told "do not impose on others what you would not want yourself." This isn't evasion; it is that rén is not a definition you can memorize but a direction you spend a life pursuing, one that shifts with the person and the situation. Confucius is teaching to the student: the same rén, but the particular obstacle lodged in you determines where you must begin.

Its most actionable form is the positive statement of rén: "Wishing to stand, help others to stand; wishing to arrive, help others to arrive." In plain terms: extend the good you want for yourself to other people. This is not a demand that you become a self-sacrificing saint; it is "taking what is near as the analogy"—starting from your own most immediate feelings and using them to feel your way into another's. Why does this matter? Because it moves the root of morality away from "obeying an external commandment" and onto "feeling for others as you feel for yourself"—you need no god-given book of law to tell you right from wrong; if you are willing to trade places in your heart, the answer is already inside you. It changes how you see the world in this: morality stops being a list of prohibitions and becomes a capacity that starts in you—an active power to help others flourish. "Is rén far off? I want rén, and behold, rén is here"—the moment you turn toward it, it is already present.

Shù (reciprocity) and the way of loyalty-and-reciprocity: humanity's earliest written Golden Rule

If you had to compress a lifetime of Confucius's teaching into a single thread, his student Zengzi supplied the answer: "The Master's Way is loyalty and reciprocity, and nothing more." Loyalty (zhōng) is doing your full part—giving your whole heart to a person or task; reciprocity (shù) is extending your own heart to gauge another's. And the definition of shù is the most famous, most endlessly quoted line in the book: "Do not impose on others what you would not want yourself."

This is what the West calls the Golden Rule—and Confucius's version is stated in the negative. It does not tell you to "actively do things for others" (which slides easily into forcing your own goodwill on them); it tells you only to refrain from harm first. That distinction matters enormously. The positive form—"do unto others what you would have done to you"—assumes the other person wants what you want; the moment they don't, kindness becomes trespass. But "do not impose what you would not want" sets an uncrossable floor—it leaves room for others to differ from you. Why does it still work today? Because it needs no religion and no shared culture; anyone can use it on the spot: before you act, put yourself in the other person's place and ask, "if it were me, could I bear this?" It changes how you see the world in this: every tangled ethical dispute can first retreat to this plainest, hardest-to-refute floor and recalibrate there.

Lǐ (ritual, propriety): giving the inner care of rén a daily, exact shape

Lǐ is easily mistaken for mere fussy etiquette. But for Confucius, lǐ is the entire set of "fitting forms of conduct" that lets a society run—from the great rites of sacrifice and mourning down to how you address your elders, receive a guest, or take your place at court. Its function is like the grammar of a language: without shared forms, the goodwill between people has no way to be expressed or read. If your love for your parents is never given the concrete shape of "attending on them morning and evening," how would anyone—how would even you—know it is there? Lǐ is the vessel that gives abstract feeling a place to rest.

But Confucius's decisive blow is this: lǐ must never spin empty. "If a person is not rén, what has he to do with ritual? If a person is not rén, what has he to do with music?" His angriest recorded moment came when the Ji clan, mere ministers of Lu, usurped the "eight-rows dance" (eight by eight, sixty-four dancers) reserved for the Son of Heaven. Confucius said: "If this can be borne, what cannot be borne?" What enraged him was not a misstep in the choreography but that the reverence and the sense of proper station behind the rite had been gutted, the form reduced to a prop for flaunting power. So lǐ and rén are two sides of one thing: rén is the lining, lǐ is the surface; ritual without rén is hypocrisy, and rén without ritual has nowhere to land. "To master yourself and return to ritual is rén"—the very discipline of curbing your private wants and bringing word and deed back onto the track of lǐ is itself the making of rén. How it changes your view of the world: refinement and sincerity are not a choice of one or the other—true refinement is sincerity ground into its right shape.

Filial love and fraternal respect (xiào-tì): why the starting point is the family

Confucius plants the root of rén in the nearest possible soil—the family. His student Youzi puts it bluntly: "Filial and fraternal love—are these not the root of rén?" Xiào is serving one's parents well; tì is honoring one's elder brothers. Why start here? Because love has an order and can be trained: a person who cannot even love the parents who raised him and the siblings he lives with day and night is a poor bet to genuinely love strangers or "all under heaven." The family is morality's first school—here you first learn to "hold another person in view," and that capacity is then pushed outward ring by ring: love of parents → kin → neighbors → the world.

Hidden here is Confucianism's sharpest break with rival schools: love is "graded." The love owed to a parent and the love owed to a stranger differ naturally in intensity, and this is taken as true human nature, not a defect. (The later Mohists argued for "impartial care"—love without gradation, loving a stranger as your own father—which Confucians denounced as violating human feeling, as "denying one's father.") Nor is xiào the later distortion of "blind obedience": Confucius plainly said that when a parent is in the wrong, the child should "remonstrate gently," not simply comply. How it changes your view of the world: grand talk of "loving all humanity" that does not begin with the nearest, most concrete person under your feet is usually empty air—morality is trained inch by inch from those around you, not proclaimed in one heroic leap.

The jūnzǐ (exemplary person): a quiet revolution in the meaning of a word

"Jūnzǐ" originally meant "son of a lord"—an aristocrat, a person of rank; it was a bloodline concept. Confucius did something with lasting reach: he swapped its basis from birth to virtue—the jūnzǐ no longer means the family you were born into but the kind of person you have cultivated yourself into. Its opposite, the "small person" (xiǎorén), likewise shifted from "commoner" to "someone of debased character." This one move flung open the door to becoming a "noble" to everyone: however humble your origin, cultivate virtue and you can become a true jūnzǐ. That is the philosophical root beneath "in teaching, no distinctions of class."

What does the jūnzǐ look like? The Analects offers a string of contrasts, each one drawing a watershed of character: "The jūnzǐ understands what is right; the small person understands what is profitable" (the exemplary person asks first whether a thing ought to be done, the small person whether it pays); "The jūnzǐ is harmonious but not conformist; the small person conforms but is not harmonious" (the one keeps concord while holding his own view, the other agrees on the surface while at odds underneath); "The jūnzǐ seeks it in himself; the small person seeks it in others" (when something goes wrong, one examines himself, the other blames everyone else); "The jūnzǐ is composed and open; the small person is forever anxious." Note that "right versus profit" is not a demand for otherworldly purity—Confucius said, "Riches and honor gained by wrong are to me as a floating cloud"; what he rejects is ill-gotten gain, not wealth as such. How it changes your view of the world: the measure of a person is not what he owns or how much, but the choices he makes when advantage is on the table, and whose door he knocks on when wronged.

Learning (xué) and "learning for oneself": cultivation as a lifelong craft

The very first line of The Analects is about learning: "To learn and to practice it in season—is this not a joy?" Note that "practice" here is not "review and memorize" but "put into action, rehearse"—Confucius's "learning" is never the accumulation of knowledge but learning how to be a person, and it counts only when it lands in doing. He states the aim of study bluntly: "The ancients studied for themselves; today people study for others"—the ancients learned to make themselves better, while people now learn to perform for an audience, chasing name and gain. Real study is learning for oneself: remaking yourself inward, not performing outward.

His method remains educational common sense: "Learning without thinking is bewilderment; thinking without learning is peril" (learn without reflecting and you are lost; reflect without learning and you are in danger—neither alone will do); "Reviewing the old, you know the new" (savor fresh meaning out of what you already know); "To know what you know and know what you do not know—that is knowing" (admitting your ignorance is itself real wisdom); "Walking with two others, I am sure to find my teacher there"; "There is no shame in asking those below you." And the highest state of learning is not grinding effort but joy: "To know it is not as good as to love it; to love it is not as good as to delight in it." How it changes your view of the world: growth is not the cashing-in of talent but a craft you can work at day by day—one that grows more savory the longer you practice it.

Governing by virtue, and rectifying names: to rule is, in the end, to order human hearts

At the heart of Confucius on politics is a counterintuitive claim: good governance works through virtue, not through punishment. He offers an image: "Govern by virtue, and you are like the Pole Star, which keeps its place while all the other stars turn toward it"—let the ruler have virtue and the people orbit him of their own accord. He draws the line between rule by virtue and rule by law with total clarity: "Lead them by decrees and keep them in line with punishments, and they will avoid the punishment but have no sense of shame; lead them by virtue and keep them in line with ritual, and they will have a sense of shame, and moreover reform from within." The first restrains behavior; the second remakes the heart.

Its companion idea is the rectification of names (zhèngmíng): "If names are not rectified, speech does not accord with truth; if speech does not accord with truth, affairs cannot succeed." The point is that when a society's "roles and titles" fall into disorder—a ruler unlike a ruler, a minister unlike a minister, a father unlike a father, a son unlike a son—everything unravels along with them. "Let the ruler be a ruler, the minister a minister, the father a father, the son a son" is not a call to freeze people in place and obey numbly; it says each role should live up to what that role ought to be and discharge the duties it carries: to be a ruler is to have a ruler's virtue and responsibility, and only then to deserve the name. The disease of politics often begins as a disease of language—a gap between name and reality. Confucius returns again and again to the ruler's example: "If he is upright himself, things get done without orders; if he is not upright, though he order, none obey"; "To govern (zhèng) is to be upright (zhèng)"—govern is upright; set yourself straight, and who would dare be crooked? How it changes your view of the world: above institutions and commands sits something more fundamental—what kind of person the one in power actually is. When the top beam is crooked, no law severe enough will keep the lower beams straight.

"Revere the spirits but keep them at a distance": a thoroughly this-life stance

Confucius lived in an age steeped in spirits, divination, and sacrifice, yet his stance is startlingly clear-eyed and restrained. Asked how to serve the spirits, he answered: "You cannot yet serve the living—how can you serve the spirits?" Asked what death is, he answered: "You do not yet know life—how can you know death?" He neither denies the spirits nor argues about them; he simply "reveres the spirits but keeps them at a distance"—holding respect while pulling attention back from the far shore to this one. "The Master did not speak of the strange, feats of strength, disorder, or spirits."

This is one of The Analects' most modern qualities: it is a thoroughly this-worldly ethics, oriented to this life and to what passes between people, placing the weight of meaning on "how to be a good person and live this one life well" rather than on an afterlife or divine revelation. It is also why, in Chinese culture, "being a good person" so often outweighs "believing in a religion." How it changes your view of the world: meaning need not be sought in the heavens—it is right here, in how you treat the people around you and in what you make of this single life.

The Distilled Spine

Twist the five hundred scattered exchanges into a single thread and The Analects is arguing one thing: a good world can grow only out of good people, one by one—and a person's goodness can be cultivated, inch by inch.

Its movement is a set of concentric circles widening from the inside out. At the very center is the heart—growing rén, that genuine care for others; the next ring out is the self—using lǐ to give that care fitting shape in word and deed, polished daily by "learning" and "self-mastery"; the next is the family—beginning with filial and fraternal love, training in one's closest relationships the capacity to "hold another in view"; and then the state and the world—the ruler carries this cultivation up to high office, winning people by virtue and by example, transforming all around him like the Pole Star. The pivot of the whole chain is "cultivating the self": governing the state and bringing peace to the world is not a separate technique but the natural magnification of the one task of becoming a good person. (The later Great Learning summed it up as "cultivate the self, order the family, govern the state, bring peace to all under heaven"—the source is right here.)

What secures the argument? Not deductive logic, nor divine authority, but two supports: first, a trust in human nature—"I want rén, and rén is here"; the seed of goodness is native to everyone, and the only question is whether you will do the work; second, a causal analogy—virtue is like water, like a magnet, like light, spreading naturally from near to far, so that "if he is upright himself, things get done without orders." Confucius himself is the most honest advocate of this vision: he "transmitted but did not create, trusting and loving the ancient," seeing himself as merely restoring the collapsed ritual-and-music order of the Zhou rather than inventing anything new; all his life he "kept trying though he knew it could not be done," attempting his ideal court by court even knowing it was near-hopeless. What The Analects finally erects is not a theoretical system but a character you can emulate—a man who made "being a good person" his life's vocation and actually lived it into a standard.

Common Misreadings & Honest Criticism

Misreading one: Confucianism = the "three bonds," blind loyalty and filial servility, absolute obedience. The likes of "if the ruler wants a minister dead, the minister must die" are the layered accretions of later ages—especially the Han thinker Dong Zhongshu's "three bonds" and the ossifications of Song–Ming Neo-Confucianism—and not necessarily Confucius's meaning. What Confucius taught was two-directional: the ruler must be ruler-like before the minister is minister-like. He explicitly said "repay a wrong with uprightness" (respond to a grievance with justice, not limitless forbearance), said one should serve a ruler by "not deceiving him, but standing up to him," and said a child should "remonstrate gently" with erring parents. Equating Confucianism with servility does the man an injustice.

Misreading two: lǐ = fussy formalism. As above, Confucius was precisely the fiercest opponent of ritual spun empty—"if a person is not rén, what has he to do with ritual?" What he wanted was measured conduct backed by genuine feeling, not soulless pageantry.

But there is also real, forceful criticism, and it deserves to be laid out honestly:

  • Conservative, backward-looking. Confucius's ideal lay in the past (the rites of Zhou); his program of "transmitting, not creating" and restoring an old order lacks, by nature, any drive to reform or critique. That later Confucianism was co-opted by dynasty after dynasty as a tool of stability control—"killing people with ritual"—is not unrelated to this conservative grain.
  • Hierarchy and the treatment of women. "Only women and small persons are hard to keep" (Book 17) has long drawn the sharpest fire—and while some scholars strain to limit its context and reference, that line, together with the whole Confucian order of male precedence, near-and-far, and above-and-below, did in fact furnish the language for later gender and class oppression. This should not be excused for the sake of a sage's honor.
  • Rén is too vague to operationalize. Confucius never fixed a definition and answered each questioner differently; the upside is flexibility, the downside is a lack of workable standards—what exactly counts as rén rests almost entirely on the cultivator's own conscience, which can decay into empty talk or self-serving reinterpretation.
  • The rival schools' head-on attacks. The Mohists found "graded love" not thoroughgoing enough and "lavish burials and long mourning" wasteful; the Daoists (Laozi and Zhuangzi) cut deeper still—"when the great Way declined, benevolence and righteousness arose"—holding that the very need to promote rén, righteousness, and ritual proves natural innocence already lost, ritual being "the thinning of loyalty and good faith, and the beginning of disorder"; the Legalists (Han Fei) mocked rule-by-virtue as impractical and remote, insisting that a chaotic age needs reward and punishment, not moral exhortation.
  • The modern reckoning. The New Culture Movement cried "down with the Confucius shop," and Lu Xun, through the mouth of his "madman," found that between the lines of a page full of "benevolence, righteousness, morality" was written "eat people"—the charge aimed chiefly at the institutionalized, man-devouring "ritual teaching," and while perhaps unfair to Confucius himself, it names a bitter fruit this body of thought really did bear in history.

One more thing to keep in mind: The Analects is not from Confucius's own hand but a record of sayings compiled by disciples and their disciples over decades and longer; the text has layers and later additions. Some chapters (notably in the second half) diverge slightly in thought from the earlier ones, and scholars have long debated their authenticity and dating. To read it is to read "the Confucius handed down by his school"—not necessarily, word for word, the Master's own voice.

The Whole Book in Ten Lines

① The root of being human is rén—a genuine care for others; "if a person is not rén, what has he to do with ritual?" Without it, the most punctilious manners are an empty shell.

② Rén is not far to reach: "I want rén, and rén is here"—the seed of goodness is native to everyone; the only question is whether you will turn toward it and do the work, now.

③ A lifetime of teaching compressed to one thread is "loyalty and reciprocity," and reciprocity's floor is universal: "do not impose on others what you would not want yourself"—before you act, stand in the other's place.

④ The positive form of rén: "wishing to stand, help others to stand; wishing to arrive, help others to arrive"—extend the good you want to others; take what is near as the analogy, feel your way into another's heart.

⑤ Ritual is the vessel of rén, rén the soul of ritual: ritual without rén is hypocrisy, rén without ritual has nowhere to land; true refinement is sincerity ground into its right shape.

⑥ Love is trained from the nearest point out—"filial and fraternal love are the root of rén"; a person who cannot love one concrete soul beside him mouths "universal love" as empty air.

⑦ The jūnzǐ looks to what is right, the small person to what pays; the jūnzǐ "seeks it in himself"—when wronged he examines himself; the small person "seeks it in others" and only blames.

⑧ Learning is for oneself, not for show—"learning without thinking is bewilderment; thinking without learning is peril"—and its summit is delight: making cultivation a lifelong craft that grows more savory with practice.

⑨ To govern is like being a person: "if he is upright himself, things get done without orders; if he is not, though he order, none obey"—above institutions and commands, what the one in power is matters most.

⑩ Return the heart to this life, this world: "you do not yet know life—how can you know death?" "you cannot yet serve the living—how can you serve the spirits?"—meaning is not in the heavens but in how you treat the people beside you and what you make of this one life.
