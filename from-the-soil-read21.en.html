From the Soil

From the Soil: The Foundations of Chinese Society · Fei Xiaotong · 1948

中文 →

In one sentence

The whole logic of how a Chinese person deals with other people — why relationships and closeness matter so much, why "personal favor" outranks abstract rule, why "sacrificing kin for a higher cause" sounds faintly monstrous while "each sweeps the snow from his own door" sounds like plain sense — is not national character and not a matter of moral rank. It is the necessary output of a particular social structure: one grown out of the land, with "familiarity" as its base coat. Fei Xiaotong captures it in a single phrase — the differential mode of association — where you never stand inside a bounded group on equal footing; instead every person is the center of ripples spreading out like a stone dropped into water, each ring fainter than the last. Grasp that image and you have read most of the source code of Chinese society.

Where it sits

Fei Xiaotong (1910–2005) is the founding figure of Chinese sociology and anthropology. He studied under the great anthropologist Bronisław Malinowski at the London School of Economics; his doctoral thesis became the famous Peasant Life in China (1939), the first time a Chinese scholar dissected his own countryside with modern fieldwork methods. From the Soil (1948) is not a field report but a collection of fourteen short essays drawn from his 1940s lectures on rural sociology. It describes no single village; instead it distills, out of countless villages, an "ideal type" of Chinese grassroots society — a deliberately purified conceptual model built to make the structure visible. The book is slim, yet for generations since it has been the one indispensable key to understanding why Chinese society is shaped the way it is.

The core claims

The book is really doing one thing: starting from the single word "soil," it derives the entire logic of Chinese society step by step, then shows how that logic comes apart under modernization. Three through-lines:

  • The base of Chinese society is its "earthbound" character. People are stuck to the land, immobile, clustered in villages, knowing one another from cradle to grave — so it is a "society of the familiar," running on familiarity and tradition rather than contract and law.
  • Its mode of organization is the "differential mode of association," not the Western "organizational mode." Not a bounded group of equals, but a web of personal ties pushed outward ring by ring from the self. Chinese morality, kinship, and politics all derive from this one structure.
  • What holds the order together is ritual-propriety and the elders, not state coercion. It runs automatically on accumulated tradition — superbly efficient in an unchanging world; yet the moment society changes fast, tradition fails, and out come the growing pains of modernity: literacy reaching the village, the shift from blood ties to territorial ties, from desire to need.

The core concepts, one by one

Earthbound society: everything grows from "soil"

Fei opens by saying the base of Chinese society is earthbound — and the word "soil," he insists, is no insult. It names the root fact precisely: people who farm cannot part from the land, land cannot be carried off, and so the farmer is pinned to one place. Unlike the herder or the trader, agriculture forces people to settle — and to settle generation after generation. Villages become near-motionless little worlds where one is born, grows old, and dies in the same spot.

Immobility has one decisive consequence: this is a "society of the familiar" — every person you deal with, you have known your whole life. The modern city, Fei notes, is a "society of strangers": you may live beside a neighbor for ten years and never learn his name, which is exactly why you need contracts, laws, black-and-white guarantees to stand in for trust. In earthbound society trust comes from familiarity — so familiar there is no need to sign anything, because the rules are known cold. His sharp formulation: the reliability of rural society rests not on respect for contracts but on submission to rules "learned" until they are unthinking instinct. This is why a Chinese person prefers to "find someone I know" to get things done, and grows awkward among strangers: the entire social instinct is configured for a world of the familiar.

Why literacy reaching the village misses the point

City people mock the villager as "stupid" — illiterate, unable to read a public notice. Fei pushes back: illiteracy is not stupidity; the villager lacks knowledge, not intelligence. Drop a city child in the fields and he may not tell wheat from chives. The real point is: in a face-to-face community where everyone meets daily, writing has nothing to do.

His reasoning is elegant. Language and writing are, at bottom, tools for conveying meaning — and imperfect tools used only when unavoidable, invented to cross the barrier of space (we are apart, so I must write) and of time (experience must pass across generations, so it must be recorded). But in earthbound society the other person is right there; a glance, a bit of dialect, even a private jargon only insiders understand, is enough — why detour through writing? So the village does without it not because its culture is low, but because the very nature of this life has no need for it. The deeper lesson: set aside the "backward-versus-advanced" verdict, and first look at a way of life's internal, self-consistent logic.

The differential mode of association: society as concentric ripples (the eye of the book)

This is the most famous and sharpest concept in the book, its beating heart. Fei's question: how do the Chinese and the Western ways of "organizing society" actually differ?

He offers two superb images. The West is the "organizational mode" — like a bundle of firewood. Straws bound into a handful, handfuls into a sheaf, sheaves into a bundle; which straw belongs to which handful, which bundle, is perfectly clear, the boundaries crisp. People are the same: you are either inside a group or outside it, and inside, everyone stands equal, equivalent in status. This is the Western association, church, company, state — the clearly outlined "group" comes first, and the individual is a member of it.

China, by contrast, is the "differential mode of association" — like the rings that spread when a stone is dropped into water. As Fei puts it (paraphrased): with the self at the center, like a stone cast into water, the social ties one forms with others are not fellow members standing level on one plane, but ripples pushed out ring by ring, each one farther and thinner than the last. Every person is the center of his own concentric circles — and no two people share a center: your web centers on you, mine on me, and no two webs coincide. Here "differential" means gradations of near and far; "mode" means the ordering of inner and outer — self first, then family, then relatives, then neighbors, ring upon ring outward, the tie ever thinner, the obligation ever lighter.

The crucial point is that these boundaries are elastic and relative. Who exactly is included in a Chinese "family"? No fixed answer — it can shrink to husband, wife, and children, or swell to embrace the whole clan. Fei cites Su Qin, the Warring States strategist: destitute and returning home, "his wife would not leave her loom, his sister-in-law would not cook for him, his parents would not speak to him"; but once he came back bearing the chancellor's seals of six states, that same sister-in-law "crawled to him on her belly" to welcome him. The very same "family" can balloon when you rise and contract when you fall — because the size of the circle depends on how far the center (you) can currently push its influence. What a Chinese calls "our own people" expands and contracts at will — the living picture of the differential mode. The traditional "five degrees of mourning" (five grades of mourning dress prescribing how heavily one grieves for kin of each distance) is simply this system of concentric circles made into an institutional ruler.

This concept unlocks a whole crowd of Chinese phenomena at once: why the Chinese work through "relationships," sort by near and far, recognize "circles"; why the line between public and private is forever blurred — because in the differential mode "public" and "private" are relative: look inward from any ring and it is public, outward and it is private. One may sacrifice the larger family for the small, the nation for the family, and each step still sounds reasonable. "Each sweeps the snow from his own door and lets the frost on another's tiles be" is here not a moral failing but the natural conclusion of the structure. Grasp the differential mode and you see: the Chinese are not without morality — their morality is forever "tailored to the person," and before deciding what rule applies to whom, one must first ask how far that person sits from the center that is the self.

Morality tied to personal ties: why there is no "treat all alike"

The differential mode directly shapes the form of Chinese morality. In the Western organizational mode, above the group hangs something that transcends the individual — God, the state, equality before the law — so morality can be universal and abstract: love all, treat everyone alike, one standard for every Tom, Dick and Harry. The very idea of "God," Fei suggests, develops in the organizational mode precisely to supply an impartial judge for the equality of all.

But the differential mode has no such transcendent group, and so morality is always the morality of personal ties, always contingent on who the other person is. He puts his finger on it: why is the central Confucian virtue, ren (humaneness / benevolence), so hard to define in the abstract? The moment Confucius is pressed on "what is ren," he breaks it into concrete virtues for concrete relations — filial piety toward parents, deference toward elder brothers, loyalty toward the ruler, good faith toward friends. Because in this system there simply is no abstract "love everyone" detached from specific relationships; everything starts from the self and is "extended from the self to others" (radiating the care one feels for oneself and one's near ones outward, ring by ring, as far as it will reach). There is no single standard identical for all people; the measuring stick is always "what is this person to me?"

This explains something deep: why the Chinese tend to find "sacrificing kin for a higher cause" a wrenching, even inhuman demand, whereas the Western moral ideal can require you to be blind to blood before the law. It is not that the Chinese are less moral, but that the two moralities have different origins — one rooted in the abstract group/God/law, the other in the concrete self. Understand this and you understand the later quarrel over the Chinese supposedly "lacking public virtue": not innate selfishness, but a morality woven of personal ties running into the modern demand of public life — that even strangers be treated alike — and finding it, naturally, unfamiliar ground.

The clan and the separation of the sexes: the family as an enterprise, not a haven

The innermost ring of the differential mode is the "family." Fei warns: the Chinese "family" and the Western family are two different things. The Western family is a bounded "small family," centered on the couple, its main function reproduction; the children grow up and split off. The Chinese "family" is instead a lineage that can grow or shrink along the paternal line, and its character is that of an enterprise — not merely a unit of living but a community that must carry a whole set of functions: production, politics, economy, religion. It exists to "run a business."

Precisely because it is a working enterprise, it demands discipline and efficiency — and discipline necessarily excludes emotion. So the main axis of the Chinese family runs along the vertical "father–son" line that can be prolonged across generations, while the horizontal "husband–wife" line becomes a secondary axis. Between husband and wife the note is "mutual respect as between guests" — cooperation and division of labor, not passionate union. This leads to one of Fei's most brilliant chapters: the separation of the sexes.

He borrows two cultural types (from Oswald Spengler): the Apollonian and the Faustian. Apollonian culture holds that the cosmos has a pre-given perfect order, and the human task is to accept and maintain it, never to challenge or create — it seeks stability. Faustian culture takes conflict as the essence of existence, sees life as ceaseless exploration, collision, overcoming, and creation — it seeks change. Earthbound society is Apollonian: stability is its supreme goal. And romantic love between man and woman is the most Faustian thing of all — an endless adventure of the soul, restless by nature, carrying the power to shatter the status quo. A society bent on stability cannot afford so explosive a passion.

Hence rural society's solution — the separation of the sexes: keep men and women institutionally apart, and discourage any deep, exploratory emotional union between them, channeling intimate feeling toward the same sex and the same generation (which is why, in the tradition, the bonds among men, or among the women of a household, often ran warmer than those between husband and wife). Marriage exists for continuing the line and for cooperation, not for passion. From which Fei draws the point: part of the price of rural society's long stability was precisely the sacrifice of emotional depth between the sexes — it bought "peace" with "separation."

Rule by ritual and the ideal of no litigation: governing by tradition, not by the state

So what keeps order in such a society of the familiar? Don't reach for the Western pair "rule of man versus rule of law," Fei says — earthbound China is a third thing: rule by ritual-propriety. "Ritual" here is not "good manners" but the socially acknowledged proper norms of conduct — the whole body of old rules for "how one ought to behave on which occasion."

Ritual and law differ fundamentally in what sustains them: law rests on the coercive force of the state (break it, and police and courts deal with you); ritual rests on "tradition" — something accumulated over generations and internalized, obeyed not from fear of punishment but from a near-reverent habit, a matter of "mastering oneself." Why does tradition work here? Because in a nearly changeless society, the experience of ancestors is a ready and reliable manual for living — forebears did it this way and came to no harm, so doing likewise cannot go wrong. Tradition thus carries its own authority; no one need explain "why," for "the old rule" is reason enough.

From this grows a distinctive orientation: the ideal of no litigation — going to court is shameful. In the Analects, Confucius says: "In hearing lawsuits I am no different from others; but what is truly needed is to bring about a state where there are no lawsuits at all." In a society ruled by ritual, a lawsuit means moral education has failed — how could decent people fall to quarreling before a magistrate? The ideal remedy is mediation: a respected elder steps in, points out publicly which old rule was broken and whose face was lost, and shames the offender into admitting fault. Disputes are settled not by adjudicating "who is right" but by teaching "who breached propriety." Fei also saw shrewdly that forcing modern law into the countryside can make things worse — the law may protect conduct the old morality despised, dismantling the old order before the villagers have learned the new rules, leaving them stranded between the two.

Four kinds of power and rule by the elders: why the emperor's writ stopped at the county

Following rule by ritual, Fei dissects the power structure of the Chinese village and proposes four kinds of power (the most theoretically accomplished part of the book):

  • Coercive power: power born of conflict and oppression — one group subduing another by force, exploitative in nature, as when the state extracts by compulsion from its subjects.
  • Consensual power: power born of the division of labor and cooperation — people mutually pledging and authorizing one another in order to work together, contractual in nature, close to modern democratic or contractual power.
  • Educative power (the power of the elders): neither coercive nor contractual, but the power of the older generation over the younger, of tradition over the individual — like a parent teaching a child: not beaten into submission, not bargained out, but the natural authority of "this is simply how it is done."
  • Power of the times: the power that appears only when society changes fast — old tradition fails, the crowd is lost, and a hero or leader arises who can point the way (saved for the "separation of name and reality" below).

Fei's insight: the actual governance of earthbound society is carried mainly by "the power of the elders," while coercive and consensual power fall to secondary roles. Why is coercive power weak? Because of governance by non-action: a peasant economy yields too thin a surplus to feed a vast bureaucracy reaching into every village, so imperial power historically stopped at the county, and village affairs were largely self-run — the economic root of "heaven is high and the emperor far away," of the emperor's writ not reaching below the county. What actually governs the villager day and night is not the emperor but the "elder" and the "old rule." In a stable society the experience an elder has amassed is the highest knowledge and the hardest authority, and the young naturally submit — this is "rule by the elders." It explains why Chinese society weighs "seniority" and reveres "age" so heavily: not moral cultivation, but a way of distributing knowledge and power.

Blood ties and territorial ties: the threshold from "status" to "contract"

In a stable earthbound society, what fixes a person's position? Fei answers: blood — the household you are born into is the status, rank, land, and occupation you inherit, son succeeding father. And territory (which village you belong to) is merely the projection of blood — you are in this village because your kin have lived in it for generations. "Born here, dying here," person and place are bound fast by blood. Newcomers can scarcely become "one of us"; they often live at the village edge, forever "guests."

Here comes a very deep observation: a blood-based society instinctively repels "doing business." You do not reckon money with your own kin — between relatives there is only "personal favor" (you give me a chicken; another day I return a basket of eggs), and the essence of personal favor is that it must never be fully squared, never settled to zero — once the account is cleared and no one owes anyone, the relationship itself is severed. But trade demands exactly "cash in one hand, goods in the other, settled on the spot," which among the familiar is awkward, even wounding. So real exchange can happen only between strangers: hence the "market" at the village edge (people carry their trade to a neutral ground where no one knows anyone), or else it is left to outside merchants.

Fei invokes the English jurist Henry Maine's famous phrase — "from status to contract" — to name the point: modern society begins precisely when people throw off the "status" blood assigns and instead form ties through freely made "contract." Moving from "blood" to "territory" is the very threshold from the earthbound to the modern — territory is the ground of a contractual society, and contract means strangers can cooperate through rational, settle-able agreements. One of the great hinges of China's modernization is stuck right on this threshold.

The separation of name and reality, and from desire to need: the old methods exposed the moment society changes

The final two essays turn the lens on change. The power of the elders, we saw, rests on "tradition being sufficient"; but once society changes fast, old experience cannot keep up with new problems, and the elders' power grows awkward — for its logic forbids open opposition (how could you say the ancestors' rule was wrong?). So change can only come on the sly: the old name and old rite are kept on the surface while the content is quietly swapped underneath — this is the separation of name and reality: the "name" (the label, the old form) persists while the "reality" (the actual content) has long since drifted. Its by-product is hypocrisy — saying one thing and meaning another, complying in appearance and defying in secret — not because people are rotten, but because a system that "forbids admitting things are changing" forces real change to march forward wearing a mask. The wider the gap between name and reality, the faster society is quietly changing. (It is here that the fourth power — "power of the times," the hero or leader who can lead people out of the impasse — finally finds its stage.)

The capstone essay, from desire to need, lifts the whole book to its highest point. Fei asks: on what does the villager manage to live so well? On desire — wanting this food, wanting to live that way, following feeling. The marvel is that these desires, filtered through generations of "cultural cultivation," have long since come to coincide with the needs of survival: people in humid regions love spicy food, which in fact helps dispel damp — yet they do not know this, they merely "like the taste." Knowing the "that" without the "why," living by the ancestors' palate, they happen to live rightly. Why does it work? Because in a slow-changing society, any desire that harmed survival was long ago mercilessly weeded out, and what remains — the "wanting" — happens to be exactly the "needing."

But modern society changes too fast, and this mechanism of "follow desire and heaven will arrange the rest" breaks down utterly — you can no longer rely on unthinking tradition; you must consciously work out what the "need" is (its function, consequences, causation) and plan accordingly. So Fei closes the book: the passage from earthbound to modern is the passage from a society ruled by "desire" to one ruled by "knowledge and planning" — from knowing the "that" to knowing the "why," from living by feeling to living with eyes open and reckoning. This is at once a liberation and a burden: heaven no longer catches you, and you must answer for your own life in full.

The distilled skeleton

The fourteen essays look scattered but form a tight chain of derivation — from one premise, "soil," it derives the whole structure, then shows how that structure collapses under modernization:

  • Premise: earthbound character → people pinned to the land, immobile → a society of the familiar (running on familiarity and tradition, not contract) → so even literacy reaching the village is useless.
  • Structure: the familiar society's mode of organization is the "differential mode" (self-centered ripples), not the "organizational mode" (a bundle of firewood) → from which derive a morality of personal ties (ren broken into filial piety, deference, loyalty, good faith) and an enterprise-like clan (father–son as main axis, the sexes kept apart).
  • What holds it: "ritual" (internalized tradition) rather than law, "the power of the elders" rather than state coercion → no litigation, governance by non-action, the emperor's writ stopping at the county.
  • Transition: once society changes fast, all of it loosens — from blood to territory (from status to contract), the separation of name and reality (hypocrisy and the power of the times arrive), from desire to need (from following feeling to rational planning).

To close the skeleton in one line: every "peculiarity" of Chinese society — working through relationships, prizing personal favor, sorting by closeness, revering elders, distrusting contract — is not a cultural gene but the reasonable product of "a society stuck to the land, mutually familiar, and nearly unchanging"; and their loosening is the chain reaction set off when modernization pulls that premise away.

Common misreadings & criticisms

  • Do not read it as "China is backward" (the first thing to correct): Fei is doing ideal-type structural analysis — "explaining why it is so," not "condemning how bad it is." Under his pen "soil" is a neutral root, not a slur; rule by ritual and the differential mode are, under their own social conditions, efficient and self-consistent. To read the book as "therefore China must wholesale Westernize" is to read it exactly backward.
  • The differential mode is an "ideal type," not a photograph: it is a deliberately purified conceptual tool; in reality the organizational and differential modes are usually mixed. Critics charge that Fei drew the East–West contrast too sharply, too neatly — real societies are not so cleanly divided.
  • The differential mode omits the dimension of "hierarchy" (the strongest scholarly criticism): later anthropologists (such as Yan Yunxiang) note that Fei foregrounded the horizontal near-and-far within the pattern while underrating the vertical high-and-low — the Chinese web is not only "how far from me" but "how far above or below me." Rank is baked into the word "differential" itself; the pattern is really two rulers, closeness and hierarchy, interwoven, and Fei gave the second too little ink.
  • Limits of era and sample: written in 1948, before the revolution, and sampled largely from the Jiangnan countryside Fei knew best, it need not cover the whole of a vast and regionally varied China. Land reform, collectivization, marketization, and urbanization have since overturned the countryside several times — the differential mode persists stubbornly in some places (today's "society of connections" and traffic in personal favors are everywhere its shadow) and has been remade in others. It must be read dynamically, not applied straight from 1948 to now.
  • "The Chinese lack public virtue" is not a moral indictment: Fei's claim that in the differential mode "public and private are relative, morality contingent on the other person" gets simplified by some readers into "the Chinese are selfish, without public spirit." That mistakes his intent: he is naming a structural cause (a morality built on personal ties colliding with a public life of strangers), not ranking a national character — which is also the measured ground of his dialogue with thinkers like Liang Shuming on "Chinese culture."

The book in ten sentences

① To understand Chinese society, seize one word first — "soil": those who farm are pinned to the land, immobile across generations, and so form a "society of the familiar," running on familiarity and tradition rather than contract and law.

② The villager's illiteracy is not "stupidity": in a face-to-face community that meets daily, writing — a tool for crossing space and time — has nothing to do; going without it reflects a life that does not need it, not a mind that falls short.

③ The eye of the book is the differential mode of association: Western society is a bundle of firewood (a bounded group of equals), Chinese society the ripples spreading from a stone dropped in water — each person the center of his own rings, pushed outward ever farther and ever thinner.

④ In the differential mode, the boundary of the circle is elastic: the "family" swells and shrinks with your fortunes (Su Qin's kin ignored him in poverty and knelt to him once he bore the seals of office); "public" and "private" are forever relative — public looking inward, private looking outward.

⑤ So Chinese morality is forever "tailored to the person": there is no abstract "love all," and "ren" is always broken into filial piety to parents, deference to elder brothers, loyalty to the ruler, good faith to friends — everything extended outward from the self, the measuring stick always "what is this person to me?"

⑥ The Chinese "family" is an "enterprise" stretching along the paternal line, not an emotional haven: father–son is the main axis (vertical), husband–wife a secondary one (horizontal); for stability's sake "separation of the sexes" suppresses romantic love, that most disruptive passion — trading "separation" for "peace."

⑦ Earthbound society is neither rule of man nor rule of law but rule by ritual: it runs on "tradition" internalized over generations, on "mastering oneself" rather than state coercion; its ideal is "no litigation" — a lawsuit means moral education has failed.

⑧ What governs the villager is not the emperor but the "elder": a peasant economy cannot feed a bureaucracy reaching into every village, so "the emperor's writ stops at the county"; the real daily power is the "educative power" of one generation over the next, of tradition over the individual — this is "rule by the elders."

⑨ From earthbound to modern is from blood to territory, from status to contract: a blood society runs on never-squared "personal favor" and repels on-the-spot trade; a modern society lets strangers cooperate through settle-able contracts — that threshold is the gate of modernization.

⑩ The moment society changes fast, the old methods are exposed: the power of the elders forbids open opposition, so change can only proceed by "the separation of name and reality" (old name kept, new reality swapped in), its by-product hypocrisy; and the ultimate turn is from "desire" to "need" — from knowing the "that" and following feeling to knowing the "why" and planning with open eyes: at once a liberation and the burden of answering for your own life in full.
