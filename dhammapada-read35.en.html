The Dhammapada

The Dhammapada · sayings of the Buddha, later compiled (Pāli Canon · Khuddaka Nikāya) · collected c. 3rd century BCE

中文 →

In one sentence

You think the world presses in on you from outside — but your mind makes everything you experience first: as the mind goes, so suffering and joy follow. The Dhammapada compresses the Buddha's core teaching into 423 verses that circle one point over and over: everything changes and cannot be held, yet you insist on clutching it (this is craving), and so there is suffering. The way out is not in the heavens and does not depend on being rescued — it lies in taming this mind of yours: live awake, practice with vigilance, and suffering can truly cease.

Where it sits

The Dhammapada is not a treatise but an anthology of verse: 423 of the most distilled lines drawn from the Buddha's forty-odd years of teaching, arranged by theme into 26 chapters ("The Pairs," "Vigilance," "The Mind," "Craving," and so on). It belongs to the Khuddaka Nikāya of the Pāli Canon and is the single most widely recited text of Theravāda Buddhism — among the most translated of all Buddhist scriptures. The title unpacks into two parts: Dhamma is the truth and teaching the Buddha revealed; pada means at once "word, step, path." Together: "the words of the truth, and the path to it." It runs no elaborate doctrinal argument; it picks the most memorable, most usable lines and says them straight — which is why, for over two thousand years, it has been countless people's first door into the Buddha's teaching.

The core claims

Across four hundred-odd verses, it grips three things:

  • The mind goes first. Your suffering and joy, your good and ill, your very fate — their source is not the outer world but the mind with which you speak and act. "Mind precedes all states; mind is their chief; they are mind-made."
  • Suffering springs from craving (taṇhā), and all things are impermanent. We clutch at what changes, disperses, and is lost — taking the impermanent for permanent, the not-self for a self — and so we are bound to the turning wheel of birth and death.
  • There is a path out, and you must walk it yourself. No one can purify you; the Buddha only points the way. Through vigilant training of the mind, ethical conduct, and clear seeing, a person can reach a peace that craving — and even death — cannot touch (nibbāna).

The core concepts, one by one

Mind precedes all (manopubbaṅgamā dhammā): first the mind, then the world

The very first verse of the whole book is also its bedrock. The Buddha says: all states are led by mind, made by mind, mastered by mind — every condition you undergo is led out in front by the mind and made by it. The two verses that follow are the book's most famous pairing:

If you speak or act with a defiled mind, suffering follows you as the wheel follows the hoof of the ox that draws the cart.
If you speak or act with a pure mind, happiness follows you like a shadow that never leaves. (paraphrased, vv. 1–2)

This is not feel-good positivity but a causal mechanism: the same event meets you whether you take it with resentment or with clarity, and the two grow utterly different aftermaths — resentment breeds more friction, more sleepless nights, more words and deeds you'll regret, and suffering grinds along behind you like a wheel; clarity lets joy trail you effortlessly, like a shadow you never have to chase. The Buddha moves the lever for "changing your fate" out of the external world and squarely back onto your own mind. It is verse one because everything that follows — vigilance, cutting craving, patience, self-mastery — is at bottom the same task: guarding and training this "leader." It changes how you see the world like this: you stop asking first "who made me this way," and instead glance back at "with what kind of mind am I meeting this, right now."

Vigilance (appamāda): the engine of the whole book

If "mind precedes all" is the foundation, appamāda — vigilance, diligence, not-being-careless — is the engine that runs the building. Tradition holds that the Buddha's very last words were on this theme. Chapter 2 is devoted to it and opens without hedging:

Vigilance is the path to the deathless; heedlessness is the path to death. The vigilant do not die; the heedless are as if already dead. (paraphrased, v. 21)

Be careful not to read it as the worldly "hustle harder, don't slack." Its meaning is closer to moment-to-moment wakefulness and awareness: knowing what you are thinking, what you are doing, and where the mind is sliding — not letting greed or anger carry you off while your back is turned. Think of the words you most regret — rarely the ones you weighed and chose, more often the ones a surge of feeling pushed out before the mind could catch up, leaving you afterward to wonder "how could I have said that." Vigilance is keeping, within that half-second, a self that still watches the self. Heedlessness (pamāda) is the opposite: the muddled, drifting, let-the-mind-run-loose state in which most of our wrongs are done — not out of deliberate malice, but because we "spaced out" for a second and it was said, it was done. The Buddha gives a very domestic image: the wise guard vigilance as their most precious treasure. Why is it the engine? Because the path is not a one-shot awakening that settles everything forever; it must be re-lit in every present moment — the instant you grow heedless is the instant you slip back. It changes how you see the world like this: you begin to sense that the real watershed of a life is not "do I understand the teaching" but "can I still stay awake in the one second when the emotion rises."

Impermanence and the three marks: only what you see clearly can you release

Chapter 20 (The Path) holds three verses later revered as the three marks of existence (tilakkhaṇa) — a distillation of the entire Buddhist view of reality:

All conditioned things are impermanent (sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā);
all conditioned things are unsatisfactory (sabbe saṅkhārā dukkhā);
all things are not-self (sabbe dhammā anattā).
When one sees this with wisdom, one turns away from suffering — this is the path to purity. (paraphrased, vv. 277–279)

Take them one at a time. Impermanence (anicca): every conditioned thing (anything assembled from conditions — a flower, a relationship, you yourself) arises and passes moment to moment; not one of them holds still. Unsatisfactoriness (dukkha): the word does not claim that life is all pain, but that it is incomplete, unreliable, un-graspable — even the best things change and disperse, so if you rest your happiness on what changes, it is bound to let you fall at least once. Not-self (anattā): the hardest and most subversive — you assume there is a permanent, unchanging, in-charge "I" inside body and mind, but it cannot be found; what you call "I" is only the momentary flow of five aggregates (form, feeling, perception, formations, consciousness), the way a stream is called "a river" though no single scoop of water is "the river."

The weight is in the last half-line: seeing impermanence, unsatisfactoriness, and not-self is not meant to sink you, but to make you "turn away" — to open the fist that has been clenched shut. Almost all our suffering comes from "wanting the impermanent to be permanent": wanting youth not to age, the beloved not to change, the good days not to end. Once you know in your bones that "it was always going to change," the sense of betrayal and robbery in losing it dissolves by half. It changes how you see the world like this: impermanence is a double-edged blade — yes, it carries off the good, but it also means no pain, no situation, is permanent — and that turns out to be the deepest consolation of all.

Craving and "the builder of the house": finding the thing that binds you

If impermanence is the "symptom," craving (taṇhā — literally "thirst," the grasping after sense-pleasure, existence, and possession) is the "root of the disease." Chapter 24 (Craving) reaches for a precise image:

Craving is like a creeping vine (māluvā); let it run and it overspreads you, chokes you. To be free, cut it at the root — uproot craving entirely, and suffering springs up no more. (paraphrased, vv. 334, 338)

The vine is worth spelling out: the māluvā is a parasitic creeper that, once it climbs a great tree, tightens ring by ring until it strangles the towering trunk — that is craving: at first only a small "I want," but follow it and it grows into a mat that binds your whole life. And the most moving cluster in the book is the Buddha's own account of the moment of awakening — his reckoning with craving, known since antiquity as the "house-breaker" verses":

Through many births in this round of wandering I sought the builder of this house and did not find him; painful is birth again and again.
O builder of the house, now you are seen! You shall build no house again — your rafters are broken, your ridgepole shattered; my mind has reached the unconditioned, and every craving is spent. (paraphrased, vv. 153–154)

Here the "house" is this body-and-mind of yours that is reborn to suffer again and again, and the "builder" is craving — the very thing that keeps dragging you back into the round, raising one "house of suffering" after another. The Buddha says "now you are seen," and there is the whole art of it: once craving is seen through, once it is named, it loses its power — for it works only so long as you cannot see it, so long as you take it for a matter-of-course "I just want it." It changes how you see the world like this: the next time a fierce "I must have this" arises, you'll ask one more question — do I truly need it, or is the "builder" trying to raise me yet another cell?

You are your own refuge (attā hi attano nātho): no one can purify you

Buddhism is often mistaken for "praying to gods and buddhas for protection." Chapter 12 (Self) cuts that misunderstanding away at a stroke:

You are your own refuge; who else could be your refuge?
By yourself evil is done, by yourself you are defiled; purity and impurity depend on yourself alone — no one can purify another. (paraphrased, vv. 160, 165)

This is the Buddha at his most "un-outsourceable": no external god, teacher, or ritual can win your freedom for you; the deeds you did and the mind you defiled can be cleaned only by you. Again and again the Buddha likens himself to one who points the way — the road is shown clearly, but every step must be your own. This lays responsibility heavily back on each person's shoulders, yet it also grants the greatest dignity and power: since purity depends wholly on you, whatever your birth, whatever you have done, the key to change has always been in your own hand — no one ever took it away. It changes how you see the world like this: you stop waiting for "someone to come save me, to change my circumstances," and accept a bleak but empowering fact — the only one who can save you is your own mind, willing to turn back and do the work.

Conquer yourself: the hardest victory, and the most worth winning

Chapter 8 (The Thousands) holds a widely loved verse that states the Buddha's order of priorities bluntly:

Though one conquer a thousand thousand men in battle, greater is the one who conquers himself — self-conquest is the highest victory. (paraphrased, v. 103)

For the Buddha, a person's true enemy is never another but one's own greed, hatred, and delusion. The same chapter runs a string of "better than…" contrasts, sharp in their values: better one day lived seeing the truth than a hundred years lived blind to it; better a single moment of inner peace than a thousand outer diversions. This pairs with "hatred ceases only by non-hatred" from Chapter 1: "He abused me, he beat me, he robbed me" — whoever harbors such thoughts, their hatred never stills; for in this world hatred is never appeased by hatred — only by non-hatred (loving-kindness) is it appeased. This is an eternal law (esa dhammo sanantano). Read together, the Buddha offers a thoroughly inward discipline: changing the world is hard, but you can always change the mind with which you meet it — and that is the highest-leverage battle of all. It changes how you see the world like this: the yardstick for "did I win today" shifts from "whom did I beat" to "did I, where I was about to lose control, hold myself in check."

Virtue as fragrance: a person's worth is measured only by conduct

Chapter 4 (Flowers) uses a run of floral images to speak of good, evil, and kamma (deeds and their consequences). The Buddha says: however fine a flower's scent, it can only travel downwind; the fragrance of virtue alone travels even against the wind — a truly virtuous person's sweetness spreads in every direction. (paraphrased, v. 54) The contrast is elegant: every worldly good — looks, wealth, fame — is like flower-scent on the wind, scattered the moment the wind shifts, and able to go only one way; but the goodness inside a person is like a fragrance no headwind can suppress, carried wherever they go, undispersed by time or misfortune.

From this the Dhammapada draws a radically egalitarian stance: a person's high or low worth is set not by birth, caste, or wealth, but by their present conduct and mind. In the rigidly stratified India of twenty-five centuries ago, that was a strikingly bold line — the Buddha insists again and again that whether one is "noble (a brahmin)" has nothing to do with the caste one was born into, only with whether one has truly cut greed and hatred and walks straight. The logic of kamma sits here too: good and bad deeds are like seeds sown, sooner or later fruiting on oneself — "do not make light of small evil, thinking it will not touch you," for drop by drop even a great jar is filled. It changes how you see the world like this: your yardstick for yourself and others shifts from "what does he possess, what has he become" to "how is he acting, what is he sowing" — because the first scatters on the wind, and only the second is what you can truly carry, and what truly counts.

The essential spine

The Dhammapada builds no system, but string its 26 chapters together and a very clear line emerges:

  • Start: the mind — all suffering and joy are led by the mind, so practice is the training of the mind (chs. 1, 3).
  • Method: vigilance — the master-key of that training is unbroken wakefulness, guarded like treasure (ch. 2).
  • Diagnosis: impermanence and craving — all things are impermanent and not-self, yet we insist on clutching them, and that craving is the "house-builder" that binds us to the wheel of birth and death (chs. 20, 24).
  • Where to begin: yourself, and self-conquest — no one can purify you; the real battlefield is within, and the greatest victory is over your own greed, hatred, and delusion (chs. 8, 12).
  • Fruit: stillness — walk the path to its end and you reach a steadiness that craving, birth-and-death, even praise and blame cannot reach (nibbāna), like a great rock unmoved by the wind.

To close the line in one sentence: what it establishes is this — suffering is not imposed by fate but produced by the mind; and being mind-made, it can be undone by the mind, whose key you have held all along.

Common misreadings & criticism

  • "All things are impermanent" is not world-weary defeatism. Many hear "all is suffering, all is empty" and assume the teaching says to give up and stop loving the world. The opposite — seeing impermanence clearly is meant to stop you betting your happiness on what will shatter, so you live more clear-eyed and less anxious about gain and loss. That blade cuts pain too: no bad situation is permanent either.
  • "Not-self (anattā)" does not mean "you don't exist." What it denies is the permanent, independent, in-charge soul-self — not the phenomenal you who is reading and feeling right now. Reading it as nihilism is one of the great historical misunderstandings of Buddhism.
  • "Dukkha" is translated too narrowly. Both "suffering" and its common renderings lean toward "pain," tempting readers to think the Buddha said life is nothing but pain. The sense is closer to "incomplete, unreliable, unable to fully satisfy" — even pleasure counts as dukkha, because it changes and is lost. Miss this and the whole text reads askew.
  • The verses are an anthology, stripped of context. Traditionally each line answered to an "occasion story" (recorded in the Dhammapada commentary) — spoken by the Buddha to a particular person, about a particular matter. Reading them as universal maxims is convenient but can flatten their original measure and audience, and can even read into the Buddha an absoluteness he did not intend.
  • There is more than one version. The one we know best is the Pāli text, but history also holds the Gāndhārī Dharmapada, the Sanskrit Udānavarga, and other "dhammapada"-type collections, differing in chapters and verse-count. The Pāli is the most popular — which is not the same as "the one original."
  • An honest criticism. The most forceful long-standing objection to this kind of teaching is that it makes liberation intensely personal and inward (tend your own mind, cut your own craving) and says almost nothing about structural injustice or how to remake the outer world. In the modern West it is often further "diluted" into motivational quotes and relaxation-mindfulness, its renunciant undertone stripped away (the goal was escape from the round of rebirth, not a better worldly life). Worth remembering as you read: it hands you an inward discipline, not a program for changing the world — you need both, but don't make one stand in for the other.

The essence in ten sentences

① The mind goes first: the mind with which you speak and act decides whether suffering or joy follows — like the wheel behind the ox, like the shadow behind the body. The lever for changing your fate is in the mind, not the circumstance.

② "Vigilance is the path to the deathless": practice is not understanding the doctrine but keeping moment-to-moment awareness, guarded like your most precious treasure — most of our wrongs are done in the second we "space out."

③ All conditioned things are impermanent, unsatisfactory, and not-self: they change, they cannot be relied on, and no permanent "I" can be found — seeing this is meant to open the fist, not to sink you.

④ The root of suffering is craving — it grows like a parasitic vine; follow it and it strangles you; to be free, cut it at the root, and suffering springs up no more.

⑤ At the moment of awakening the Buddha said to craving: "O builder of the house, now you are seen — you shall build no house again"; once craving is seen through, it loses the power to bind you.

⑥ "You are your own refuge": purity or impurity depends on you alone; no one can purify another. The Buddha only points the way — the road is yours to walk.

⑦ "Though one conquer a thousand thousand men in battle, greater is the one who conquers himself" — the real enemy is your own greed, hatred, and delusion, and the highest victory is that inward one.

⑧ "Hatred is never appeased by hatred; only by non-hatred is it appeased — this is an eternal law" — returning hate for hate only feeds the fire.

⑨ The fragrance of virtue travels even against the wind, surpassing every flower's scent; a person's true worth is measured not by birth but by their conduct and their mind. (paraphrased, ch. 4)

⑩ At the end of the road is a stillness that praise and blame cannot stir, that birth-and-death cannot reach — like a solid rock unmoved by the eight winds. (paraphrased, ch. 6)
