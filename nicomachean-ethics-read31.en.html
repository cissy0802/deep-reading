Nicomachean Ethics

Aristotle · c. 340 BCE

中文 →

In One Sentence

How should a whole life be lived so that it counts as having gone well? Aristotle's answer is not pleasure, wealth, or fame, but "the excellent, virtuous exercise of what is distinctively human, sustained across a complete life"—and virtue is neither an inborn temperament nor a doctrine you merely grasp with your head, but a stable state you build into your character by repeatedly doing the right thing until it becomes second nature. What this book teaches, in the end, is a craft: how to become a good person, and thereby live well.

Coordinates

Aristotle was Plato's student, Alexander the Great's tutor, and the first person in Western history to carve knowledge into disciplines and found nearly all of them (logic, physics, biology, politics, rhetoric…). The Nicomachean Ethics is his mature course of lectures on ethics—the title likely from his son (or editor) Nicomachus. Its place is foundational: it is the founding work of Western virtue ethics and the fountainhead of the eudaimonist tradition. For two thousand years, every serious inquiry into "how should one live"—from Aquinas's scholasticism to the virtue-ethics revival launched in recent decades by Anscombe and MacIntyre—has had to reckon with it. Note: this is a set of lecture notes, not a polished treatise; it reads as rough, repetitive, and colloquial—but for that very reason it stays unusually close to how he actually thought.

Core Claims

Its ten books bite down on three things:

  • Every action aims at some good, and among these goods there is one final good, pursued for its own sake—happiness (eudaimonia). Everything else (money, health, honor) we want for its sake; it we want for the sake of nothing further.
  • Happiness is not a feeling or a matter of luck, but an activity—the soul's activity in accordance with virtue, across a complete life. "One swallow does not make a summer"; nor does one good day make a happy life.
  • Virtue (aretē) comes in two kinds: virtue of character, grown through habit and landing on a "mean"; and virtue of intellect, from teaching—the chief of which is practical wisdom, the helmsman that aims you at the right point in each concrete situation.

The Core Concepts, One by One

Eudaimonia: not "feeling good," but "flourishing"

This is the book's bedrock, and the word modern readers most easily misread. It is usually rendered "happiness," but that's off. Eudaimonia literally means "having a good guardian spirit (daimon)"; its real sense is "flourishing, thriving, living well and doing well"—an objective verdict on an entire life, not some momentary subjective glow. A person asleep, or under anesthesia, feels no pain and is not exactly unhappy—yet you would not say he is "living a flourishing life," because happiness is an activity, not a mood.

Aristotle first corners it with a move: everything has a "for the sake of which" (a telos), and these ends nest inside one another—you exercise for health, health for the sake of doing things, doing things for the sake of… Eventually there must be an end pursued "for itself and never for anything else," or the whole chain of wanting dangles in the void. That final end must meet two conditions: it must be "final"—always an end, never a means; and "self-sufficient"—by itself it makes a life complete, lacking nothing. Money fails (it's for other things); honor fails (it depends on those who grant it, it's external); pleasure isn't enough (cattle pursue pleasure too). The only thing that meets both is happiness. The power of this step is that it turns "the meaning of a life" from a vague, sentimental question into a structured, arguable one.

The function argument (ergon): first, what is a human being for?

Knowing happiness is the final end isn't enough—what actually counts as "flourishing"? Here Aristotle makes the book's pivotal inference, the function argument. His logic: to judge whether anything is good, look at how well it performs its "characteristic function (ergon—the thing that is proper to it and to nothing else)." A good flute-player is one who does the flute-player's proper business—playing—excellently; a good eye is one that sees clearly. So what is a human's ergon? Not "being alive" (plants live too), not "sensing" (animals sense too), but "the activity of the part of the soul that has reason (logos)"—living and guiding one's conduct by reason is the function proper to humans alone.

The conclusion follows cleanly: the human good = the soul's activity in accordance with virtue (doing the activity "well" is doing it in accordance with its excellence/virtue); and if there is more than one virtue, in accordance with the best and most complete; and across "a complete life." This argument is the book's axle: it lands the abstract end "happiness" as an operational definition—to live a life well is to exercise, stably and excellently, that rational function proper to human beings. It also seeds the book's division of labor: since the soul has a rational and a non-rational part, virtue splits in two—virtue of character (which governs non-rational desire) and virtue of intellect (which governs reason itself).

Virtue is habit (aretē / ethos): good people are made, not merely convinced

This is the book's most practical—and most counterintuitive—insight. Virtues of character (courage, temperance, generosity, justice) are none of them inborn, and none of them acquired just by grasping a doctrine; they are all grown through "habit (ethos)." Aristotle plays on the etymology: the word for "moral (ēthikē)" comes from "habit (ethos)." What is natural cannot be retrained (throw a stone upward ten thousand times and it will never learn to rise on its own); yet virtue can be grown by repeated practice—proof that it is not nature but "second nature."

His famous line: "We become just by doing just acts, temperate by doing temperate acts, brave by doing brave acts." The order is reversed—you do not first possess virtue and then act; rather, you act again and again, and only in the acting does virtue get stamped into a stable state (hexis, a "disposition"). It works exactly like a craft: you don't learn the lyre first and then play; you become a player by playing, and a builder by building. So he is emphatic: "It makes no small difference, then, whether we form habits of one kind or another from our very youth; it makes a very great difference, or rather all the difference." This reframes what it takes to get better: reading ethics and forming resolutions won't do it—you must act, until you act without deliberation and even take pleasure in it. Only then is it true virtue: "the sign of a good character is taking pleasure in doing what is noble." It also explains why upbringing, environment, and companions are so decisive: you are shaped by whatever conduct is repeated on you.

The mean (mesotēs): virtue as the "just right" between two extremes

This is the book's most famous concept—and the one most often mangled. Aristotle says: every virtue of character is a "mean" between two vices, one of excess and one of deficiency. Feelings and actions admit of too much, too little, or just right—and the "just right" amount is the virtue. His own examples make it plain: facing danger, excess is rashness, deficiency is cowardice, and the mean is courage; facing bodily pleasures, excess is self-indulgence, deficiency is insensibility, the mean is temperance; with money, excess is prodigality, deficiency is stinginess, the mean is generosity. Honor, anger, sociability, wit—each has its two extremes and its midpoint.

Three things must be pinned down, or this collapses into a vulgar "always compromise, always split the difference"—the very misreading to guard against. First, the mean is not an arithmetic midpoint but a mean "relative to us." His analogy: in diet, the ration right for the wrestler Milo is far too much for a beginner—the "mean" depends on the person and the case, not a one-size average. Second, the mean is about hitting feeling and action "at the right time, toward the right objects, for the right end, in the right way"—and hitting that point is extremely hard: "it is easy to do wrong, but to hit the mean is hard, as hitting the bull's-eye is hard and missing is easy." So the mean is not mediocrity; on the contrary, "with respect to what is best and right, virtue is an extreme." Third, not everything has a mean. Some feelings and actions are bad in themselves—spite, envy, theft, murder, adultery—and there is no "doing them in moderation"; to do them at all is to go wrong. The mean is a measuring rod, not a license issued to every act.

Practical wisdom (phronesis): the helmsman that aims you at the mean

Here's the problem: if the mean is "relative to person and situation," with no formula, then what actually finds it in each concrete moment? The answer is the most important virtue of intellect—phronesis (practical wisdom, prudence). It is "the capacity to deliberate well about what is good and bad for a human being, and to act on it." Note how it differs from cleverness and from knowledge: it does not concern the eternal, unchanging truths of mathematics (that's theoretical wisdom, sophia); it concerns "things that can be otherwise, that our action can change"—the concrete human matters of what to say and do, here and now, to this person.

Aristotle is down-to-earth about it: practical wisdom cannot rest on youthful cleverness alone; it needs experience—because it handles particulars, and a grip on particulars comes only from having lived. That's why "young men may become geometers, but hardly ever practically wise." The crucial move is that he binds virtue of character and practical wisdom together as mutual conditions: "It is not possible to be good in the full sense without practical wisdom, nor practically wise without virtue of character." Why? Virtue of character sets the end (what to aim at); practical wisdom finds the right means and measure to reach it. Without the former, cleverness degrades into "clever wickedness (cunning)"; without the latter, good intentions botch the deed. Together, they make a whole good person who both knows where to go and knows how to get there in this moment. This upgrades "being good" from reciting moral rules into a practical craft that demands experience, judgment, and on-the-spot proportion.

Weakness of will (akrasia): the strange fact of "knowing better and doing worse"

This is the book's finest piece of reasoning, tackling a bind everyone has hit: why do we so often know perfectly well what is right, and still fail to do it? The Greek is akrasia (incontinence, weakness of will)—your mouth knows to eat less, sleep earlier, start writing, while your body honestly does the opposite. Socrates, teacher of Aristotle's teacher, made a startling claim: no one does wrong knowingly; all wrongdoing is ignorance—if you truly "knew" it was bad, you couldn't do it, and doing it just shows you didn't really know. That denies weakness of will exists—yet it plainly clashes with experience.

Aristotle's handling is delicate: he partly grants Socrates and partly rescues common sense. He distinguishes two senses of "knowing": knowledge actively in use, being drawn on right now, and knowledge possessed but, like that of a drunk or a sleeper, not activated. The weak-willed person has the latter—his knowledge is momentarily "clouded and shelved" under the blow of desire, like an actor mouthing lines or a drunk reciting verse: on the lips, but not truly lived. He also dissects it with a "practical syllogism": the major premise ("sweets shouldn't be eaten") is present, but the minor ("this cake is a sweet") gets overridden by strong desire and fails to connect, so the conclusion (don't eat) never comes out. So a person doesn't do wrong while "knowing everything"; at the decisive instant, desire makes the relevant knowledge "drop the connection." The brilliance of this analysis: it is neither naïvely "understanding guarantees doing" nor bleakly "knowing and doing are forever split"—it locates exactly where the crack is: the work of virtue is to train knowledge until it stays connected even when desire is strongest—built into habit, made automatic. (It rhymes, across two millennia, with Kahneman's insight that knowing a bias is not enough to correct it.)

Friendship (philia): the good life needs "another self"

Two whole books (VIII and IX) are given to friendship—a measure of its weight. Philia is broader than "friendship": it spans friends, kin, partners, comrades, even the bond between fellow citizens. Aristotle sorts it into three kinds by what you actually love in the other: ① friendship of utility (you really love the benefit he brings); ② friendship of pleasure (you love the fun he provides); ③ friendship of character/virtue (you love the person himself, for being good). The first two are fragile: when the benefit dries up or the fun fades, the bond dissolves—which is why "the friendships of the young shift fast, and those of the old are mostly for advantage." Only the third is complete friendship: loving a person for who he is and wishing him well for his own sake; this bond is stable, lasting, and incidentally useful and pleasant too.

He has a deep line: a friend is "another self (another oneself)." Why does a good person, already self-sufficient, need friends? Because knowing oneself is hard, and one can see oneself clearly in the "mirror" of a friend; a friend's good deeds delight you as your own do; and no one, however self-sufficient, would choose to have every good thing on the condition of being alone. He even says the finest thing you can wish a friend is that he remain the good person he is—not that he be raised (say) to a god, for then he'd no longer be your friend. This section expands ethics from "how one person cultivates himself" into "a human is by nature a political/social animal, and a good life is necessarily lived with others, filled with love and friendship"—happiness was never a solitary affair.

Contemplation (theoria): the highest happiness is to think as the gods do

In the final book (X), Aristotle advances the book's highest and most contested claim. Happiness, he has said, is "activity in accordance with virtue"—but virtue is not one thing, so which activity is the highest happiness? His answer: contemplation (theoria—the pure, theoretical activity of the intellect gazing on eternal truths). The reasons interlock: intellect (nous) is the highest, most divine part of us; its objects (truth, eternal things) are the noblest; this activity is the most continuous, the most self-sufficient (the philosopher can think alone, whereas justice and courage need objects and opponents), the least dependent on externals, pleasant in itself and pursued for no further end—it comes closest to "the life of a god." So he says: "We must not follow those who advise us to think only of human things because we are human; rather, we must, so far as we can, make ourselves immortal, and strain every nerve to live in accordance with the best thing in us."

But he does not overstate it. He concedes this is a life "higher than the human"—we live it not insofar as we are human, but by the trace of the divine in us; and as complete human beings, made of bodies and feelings and living among others, we also need the life of character-virtue (justice, courage, friendship) and the "external goods" of health, friends, moderate means. So the highest happiness is contemplation, but a real person's good life is a composite: contemplation at the summit, virtuous practice as the body. This ending lifts a whole book about human virtue to a near-religious height: a human's best moments are when he briefly slips free of a mortal's trivia and touches the eternal.

The Distilled Spine

Say the thing this fat book keeps circling, once and clearly, as a chain:

  • The end: every action aims at some good, and above all goods is one that is "for its own sake and self-sufficient"—happiness (eudaimonia), i.e. "living well and doing well."
  • The definition: the function argument lands it—the function proper to humans is "the rational activity of the soul," so happiness = the soul's activity in accordance with virtue, across a life.
  • Two virtues: virtue of character (courage, temperance, justice…) grown by habit, landing on the mean; virtue of intellect from teaching, chiefly practical wisdom—which aims character-virtue at the mean in each concrete case; neither works without the other.
  • The puzzle: why "knowing" doesn't guarantee "doing" (akrasia)—the work is to train knowledge to stay connected when desire is strong.
  • The community: the good life needs friendship—a friend is "another self"; humans are political animals, and happiness is shared.
  • The summit: the highest happiness is contemplation (theoria), closest to the divine; but a whole human still needs virtuous practice and external goods—summit and body as one.

One sentence to close the chain: happiness is not a state you "have" but a person you "become"—through a lifetime of activity in accordance with virtue, exercising what is human in you to its utmost.

Common Misreadings & Criticisms

  • "The mean = be moderate about everything, split every difference" (the #1 misreading): wrong. The mean is the extremely hard-to-hit bull's-eye of "doing the right thing at the right time, in the right way"—with respect to excellence it is an extreme; and some acts (murder, adultery, spite) have no mean at all—to do them is simply to err. It is precision, not compromise.
  • Reading eudaimonia as subjective "happiness": Aristotle explicitly rejects equating happiness with pleasure—that's "a life fit for cattle." Happiness is an objective verdict on a whole life (flourishing); pleasure is a byproduct that supervenes on good activity, not the thing itself.
  • The circularity and the "luck" worry: critics note that "virtue is the mean the practically wise person would choose," while "the practically wise person just is the virtuous one"—the definition looks circular, amounting to "do as the good person does," giving outsiders no independent criterion. More bitingly, he himself concedes happiness needs external goods and luck—health, wealth, good birth, good friends, even looks and children; a man like Priam of Troy, struck by catastrophe in old age, can hardly be called to have lived a happy life. That sits in unresolved tension with "happiness should be self-sufficient, in your own hands"—a tension he never fully closes.
  • Does the function argument hold? Moderns ask: what licenses saying "a human has a function"? A knife has one (we made it to cut), but a naturally evolved human need not have a built-in "purpose"; "reason is the human's proper function" may smuggle a value into a "fact" (the Humean is-to-ought worry).
  • The fault-line in Book X (a long scholarly dispute): Books I–IX weight character-virtue, friendship, and political life so heavily, yet Book X says the highest happiness is solitary contemplation—are these two ideals "compatible (contemplation as crown, virtue as body)" or "at war (contemplation dominant, the rest demoted to means)"? This is the famous "inclusive vs. dominant" debate, still unsettled. Elevating contemplation to "near-divine" is also faulted as departing from his own consistently grounded, human-scaled temper.
  • Be honest about the period limits: Aristotle treated slavery and the subordination of women as "natural," holding that a slave is by nature a "living tool" and that a woman's rational faculty "lacks authority." His "who can live a happy life" carries exclusionary class and gender premises. Reading him today means taking the insight without defending the blind spots.
  • Why it caught fire again: in the mid-20th century Anscombe's essay, then MacIntyre's After Virtue, drove a "virtue ethics" revival—against Kantian "just follow the rule" and utilitarian "just tally the consequences," it pulled the question back to Aristotle's framing: not "which rule should I obey," but "what kind of person should I become." That is the real reason it stays evergreen.

The Whole Book in Ten Sentences

① Every action is for some good, and above them all is a final good—happiness (eudaimonia); it alone is "always an end, never a means, and by itself makes a life complete."

② Happiness is not a feeling or luck, but an "activity"—the soul's activity in accordance with virtue, across a whole life: one swallow does not make a summer, nor one good day a happy life.

③ To know what "living well" is, look at the human's proper function: not being alive, not sensing, but "living by reason"—exercising that function excellently is happiness (the function argument).

④ No virtue of character is inborn; all come by habit: we become just by doing just acts, brave by doing brave acts; virtue is "made," not "understood."

⑤ "What habits we form from youth makes no small difference—it makes all the difference": you are shaped into whatever conduct is repeated on you.

⑥ Virtue is the mean between two vices (excess and deficiency): courage between rashness and cowardice—but the mean is not compromise, it is the extremely hard bull's-eye of "the right thing, at the right time, in the right way."

⑦ Some acts have no mean: spite, theft, murder, adultery—there is no "doing them in moderation"; to do them is to err. The mean is a measuring rod, not a license.

⑧ Aiming at the mean takes "practical wisdom (phronesis)"—which needs experience and on-the-spot judgment; you cannot be truly good without it, nor have it without virtue of character: the two are mutual conditions.

⑨ People often "know better and act worse" (weakness of will)—not from ignorance, but because at the decisive instant desire makes knowledge "drop the connection"; the work is to train it to hold even under strong desire.

⑩ A good life needs friendship (a friend is "another self") and aspires to the highest contemplation—a human's best moments are slipping free of mortal trivia to touch the eternal: strain every nerve to live in accordance with the best thing in you.
