What Life Should Mean to You

What Life Should Mean to You · Alfred Adler · 1931

中文 →

In one sentence

Everyone starts life from a felt "I'm not good enough," and everyone spends a lifetime straining to push themselves from a minus toward a plus — but what decides who you become is not how strong that drive is, it's which way you point it: toward "outdoing others, proving I'm superior," or toward "being with others, contributing something to them." The first is a road that only grows lonelier; the second is what Adler means by rising above.

Coordinates

Adler (1870–1937) was an Austrian psychiatrist, ranked with Freud and Jung as one of the three founders of depth psychology. He was once a core member of Freud's circle, but broke with him publicly in 1911 — because he refused to believe that "sex" was the master switch behind all human behavior. He went his own way and founded Individual Psychology. The name is easily misread: "individual" here carries its Latin root individuum, "indivisible" — it means "a person is one indivisible whole, running toward a single goal," not "individualism." This 1931 book, aimed at ordinary readers, is his summing-up: it lays out his entire system — inferiority, striving for superiority, social interest, the tasks of life — in the plainest language he could manage.

Core claims

The whole book rests on three nails:

  • People are pulled forward by a future goal, not pushed from behind by a past wound. This is his deepest split from Freud: don't ask "what caused you to become this," ask "what are you trying to achieve by it."
  • Inferiority is the shared starting point and engine of every human being — the question is never "is there inferiority," but "how do you respond to it": do you use it as an excuse to lie down, or as fuel to climb.
  • There is only one yardstick for whether a person is healthy and living rightly: social interest — whether they are willing to cooperate with others and contribute to the community. The meaning of life, in the end, is one word: contribution.

The core concepts, one by one

Inferiority feeling: the engine we all carry

First, dismantle the biggest misunderstanding: the inferiority Adler means is not a defect — it is fuel. His logic is hard: every one of us grew up from an infant who was small, weak, and unable to do anything. In your first years you were surrounded by giants (adults), dependent on others for everything. This condition of "I am less than everything around me" is universal and inborn, not a flaw belonging only to the unlucky. Precisely because everyone starts from a minus sign, everyone has that drive to grow stronger, to reach upward.

Adler himself was a living specimen: as a child he had rickets, nearly died of pneumonia at four, and felt outclassed at every turn by his healthy, lively older brother — it was exactly this firsthand "not good enough" that forced out his life's theory. So his famous line runs (paraphrased): "To be human is to feel inferior." Once you take that in, your angle on the world shifts: the man who trains obsessively, the student who must rank first, the person who loves to show off — under all of them the same engine is running. Inferiority is never a disease in itself; it is the prime mover of all human progress. It turns to poison only when it is put to the wrong use.

Striving for superiority and compensation: lunging from minus toward plus

If everyone starts from inferiority, then every human life has a single master direction: striving for superiority — Adler also calls it striving for perfection, meaning the strenuous move from a situation felt as "lacking (minus)" toward one felt as "complete (plus)." Note this is far wider than "wanting to beat other people": it is the upward, forward tension of life itself, a basic fact of being alive. This is Adler's second break from Freud: where Freud said people are pushed by the sexual drive (libido), Adler said no — people are pulled by a will to rise.

When that drive lands on a concrete weakness, it becomes compensation: wherever a person is weak, in body or mind, they instinctively work to make it up — there, or somewhere else. The classic case is Demosthenes of ancient Greece — a natural stutterer who trained his voice against the sea with pebbles in his mouth, and became Athens' greatest orator. The frail child grows into an athlete; the near-sighted child becomes a painter. A weakness need not drag you down; often it is exactly what forces out your strongest side. But compensation can also overshoot into overcompensation: to cover one patch of inferiority, a person may go too far — a child long looked down upon may grow into a tyrant who feels safe only by trampling everyone. Once you see this layer, you can read, behind other people's bravado (and your own), the un-soothed fear underneath.

Teleology and the fictional final goal: you are pulled by the future, not pushed by the past

This is the most counterintuitive — and most liberating — move in the book. Freud was a thinker of causality: you fear people today because you were bitten in childhood — the past determines the present. Adler flips it into teleology: you fear people today not "because of" the past, but "in order to" serve some purpose — for instance, "fear" gives you a legitimate way to dodge socializing, so you never have to risk being rejected. The very same childhood can be used by one person as a reason to lie down and by another as a step to rise — what decides your fate is never the experience itself, but the meaning you assign to that experience (this line is Adler's signature, and the ultimate source of every later "you can change" psychology).

And what is that "future" pulling you? It is the fictional final goal each of us carries — an ideal self-portrait of "once I become that, everything will be fine." It is mostly set half-consciously around age four or five, and it isn't real (hence "fictional," a word borrowed from the philosopher Vaihinger's "as if": a person lives toward a made-up goal much as one follows a formula known to be only an assumption). Say, "I will become someone absolutely safe, whom no one can hurt." Once that invisible goal is fixed, everything afterward — what you choose, what you avoid, whom you love or hate — quietly serves it. This yields Adler's boldest conviction: a person is not a passive product of heredity plus environment; a person has a "creative self" that actively interprets, gives meaning, and chooses the direction to run toward. Your way of seeing the world turns over completely: you stop asking "why am I like this" (digging backward, growing more helpless) and start asking "what am I doing this for" (looking forward, changeable right now).

Style of life: a person is a tune, not a heap of notes

Style of life is Adler's central integrating concept: a person's unique, unified, whole way of pursuing that final goal — how they see themselves, see the world, and what playbook they use against every problem. The keyword is "unified": Adler insisted a person is an indivisible whole. Your every act, every fragment of a dream, every slip of the tongue may look scattered, but they are all playing the same tune, all pointing to the same goal. Just as every work of one composer bears his signature, all of your behavior is stamped with your style of life.

The main melody of that tune is largely composed by age four or five — by then a child has already formed, from their own situation, a fixed view of "who I am, whether the world is safe, and how to live," and most of a life is spent circling inside that frame. To glimpse a person's style of life, Adler had a famous tool: early recollections. The few events you "happen" to keep from a vast childhood are, he held, no accident — you retain exactly those that fit your present outlook on life. Someone whose first memory is "my mother let go and I fell" and someone who recalls "the first day of school, so many new things around" are viewing life through two utterly different filters. This makes clear why change is hard: not because of some single bad habit, but because what has to move is the whole tune you've been singing since childhood.

Social interest (Gemeinschaftsgefühl): the crown of the book

If you remember only one word of Adler, make it this one. Social interest (German Gemeinschaftsgefühl, literally "community feeling") is a person's sense of connection and belonging to others and to humanity as a whole, and the disposition to cooperate and contribute — in plain terms, the feeling of "carrying others in your heart, wanting to join hands with people, wanting to add something for everyone." Adler raised it as high as it can go: it is the sole measure of all mental health.

Why? Because the "striving for superiority" drive is itself neutral; it can be pointed two ways. Turned toward social interest, the superiority you seek lands on contribution to the community — you want to grow stronger so as to love, work, and create better, and that road grows ever wider and warmer. Turned the other way (Adler calls it "personal superiority"), what you seek becomes standing over others — treating people as ladders, rivals, threats — and that road grows narrower and colder, ending in total isolation. Adler declares that all failures — neurotics, criminals, alcoholics, problem children — share a single root: a lack of social interest, a "concern only for oneself." This yardstick is ruthless and enormously practical: next time you judge whether some act or choice leads you well or badly, don't ask "what's in it for me," ask "does this connect me more to others, or isolate me more from them."

The three life tasks: the exam of life has only three questions

Adler says we are bound by three iron facts: we live on this one planet of limited resources, we are not the only humans, and we come in two sexes. These three constraints force out exactly the three great questions life must answer: ①work (occupation) — how to make a living and create on this planet; ②fellowship (friendship / society) — how to get along and cooperate with others; ③love (marriage / the two sexes) — how to build the most intimate bond with another person, and carry life onward.

These three share one thing: not one can be solved by "one person" — each demands cooperation, each demands social interest. So they are precisely the touchstone of social interest. A person short on it, facing these three, instinctively resorts to evasion — but rarely by conceding outright. Instead they play a trick Adler saw through with great precision: the "yes-but." "I'd love to find a partner, but I'm too busy"; "I could have succeeded, but my family was poor back then" — a plausible "but" that lifts you out of the exam hall while preserving the dignity of "I'm actually capable, I just didn't go." The symptoms of neurosis (anxiety, blushing, insomnia), in Adler's eyes, are often just such a "legitimate deserter's certificate": they block for you the very life task you dare not face. See this, and you can catch your own exquisite "buts" — they are not obstacles, they are the exemption passes you quietly issue yourself.

The inferiority complex and the superiority complex: two faces of one coin

Be careful not to confuse "inferiority feeling" with "inferiority complex" — this is the book's most crucial distinction. The inferiority feeling is universal and healthy, the engine. The inferiority complex is that feeling gone rancid: when a person, facing a problem, no longer uses the inferiority as motive to solve it, but is crushed by it, lies down on the spot, and treats it as a permanent excuse — "I'm just no good by nature, so don't count on me." Now inferiority has gone from fuel to paralysis.

The superiority complex is the back of the same coin: a person deep in inferiority who won't make it up by honest means turns instead to putting on an air of being above everyone, to cover it — boasting, arrogance, belittling others, quick anger, a compulsion to control everything. Adler puts it in one line: the more ferociously someone displays superiority, the deeper the inferiority usually hidden beneath. The tyrant who must top everyone, the man who brags to whoever he meets — these are not truly strong; they are too afraid of being seen as weak. Put on this X-ray lens and your reading of people sharpens at once: aggression is often fear in disguise, and arrogance is often the mask of inferiority.

The pampered and the neglected child: how social interest gets stunted

If social interest is the key to everything, where does it come from and how does it get stunted? Adler points to childhood, and names two most dangerous kinds of upbringing. The pampered child: everything done for them, every wish granted, made the little sun of the household. What they learn is "the whole world should revolve around me," and the moment they step outside and find others won't wait on them, they feel monstrously wronged and full of hostility — they never had a chance to learn this thing called cooperation. Adler calls being pampered the most dangerous situation of all, because it systematically castrates a child's social interest. The other kind is the neglected or hated child: never having tasted what love and cooperation feel like, they simply don't believe goodwill exists in the world, treating all human dealings as a cold battlefield, guarded at every turn.

Adler also discusses birth order as one facet of these family situations: the firstborn, who once had the parents to himself and was then "dethroned" by siblings, tends to prize authority and order; the youngest, coddled by all yet forever chasing those ahead, may be either fiercely ambitious or, conversely, discouraged; the only child, long among adults with no peer rivalry, may be over-mature or over-dependent. But hold onto Adler's bottom line: birth order is only a "situation," not a "destiny" — what truly shapes a child is always how they interpret their situation. (This is also where later empirical criticism concentrates; see below.) This view changes how you see the family: the core task of raising a child is never "to satisfy the child," but "to teach the child to cooperate" — to slowly adjust that lamp in their heart that lights only themselves until it can also light up others.

The distilled skeleton

Compress what the whole book keeps circling into a single line: everyone starts from inferiority (the start) → so everyone strives for superiority for life (the engine) → but that drive is steered by a fictional goal set in childhood and hardens into a unified style of life (the steering wheel) → and there are only two directions: toward "outdoing others" or toward "contributing to the community" → the latter (social interest) is the sole yardstick of mental health, and the answer to the meaning of life.

  • The start: inferiority is universal, healthy fuel — not a defect.
  • The engine: everyone lunges from minus toward plus (striving for superiority + compensation).
  • The steering wheel: teleology — you are pulled by a future fictional goal, not pushed by the past; that orientation hardens into your unique style of life, largely fixed by age four or five.
  • The fork: the engine pointed wrong yields the "inferiority / superiority complex"; the three life tasks (work, fellowship, love) are the exam that tests whether you've pointed it right.
  • The answer: social interest — the willingness to cooperate and contribute — is the only measure of health; the meaning of life is contribution.

Common misreadings & criticism

  • The misreading most worth correcting: reading the Chinese title as "conquer inferiority, chase superiority." Adler means nearly the opposite — the very "standing over others" kind of superiority is what he warns against; what he prizes is turning the force of inferiority toward "contribution." The "rising above" here is not besting other people, it is rising above the small self that cares only for itself.
  • The empirical dispute over birth order (the one most worth knowing): Adler's claims about the character of firstborns / youngest / only children spread far and wide, but later large-sample studies (e.g., Damian & Roberts 2015, Rohrer et al., analyzing hundreds of thousands of people) largely find that birth order has almost no stable effect on personality, and only a very faint effect on IQ. Discount such claims heavily — treat them as suggestive observation, not settled science.
  • The concepts are too soft, hard to falsify: critics (especially from experimental psychology) note that "social interest," "style of life," and "fictional goal" are vaguely defined, can explain almost anything, and therefore verify almost nothing — they sound like profound common sense but lack a testable core. Explaining everything by "teleology" also risks after-the-fact rationalizing.
  • "Health = social interest" smuggles in a value judgment: making "willingness to contribute to the collective" the sole criterion of mental health mixes in an ethical claim, not a purely scientific conclusion — it reads more like a good philosophy of "how one should live" than a measurable diagnosis.
  • Too optimistic, too voluntarist: Adler attributes nearly everything to "the meaning you assign to experience" and "you can choose to change," and is criticized for underrating the unconscious, the biological base, and the weight of real trauma — not every predicament unlocks with "reframe it and summon courage."
  • Yet its vitality is astonishing: precisely because of the conviction that "what decides you is not the experience but the meaning you give it," Adler became the grandfather of modern self-help psychology and cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), and deeply influenced Maslow's humanism. In recent years the bestseller The Courage to Be Disliked made Adler a phenomenon again — but note that many of that book's hard-edged formulas ("everything is a choice made now") push Adler into a more extreme, more motivational version, not necessarily his own.

Ten sentences, distilled

① To be human is to feel inferior — everyone grows up from a small, weak infant, and "not good enough" is the universal starting line; inferiority is not a defect, it is the engine of all human progress.

② So every human being strains in one direction for life: from "feeling lacking" toward "feeling complete" (striving for superiority); wherever you're weak you want to make it up (compensation) — and a weakness is often exactly what forces out your strongest side.

③ You are not pushed from behind by the past, but pulled from ahead by the future: what decides your fate is never the experience itself, but the meaning you assign to it.

④ Each of us carries a "fictional final goal" set half-consciously by age four or five, and every later choice quietly serves it; yet a person has a "creative self" that can rewrite that direction — which is why people can change.

⑤ A person is a tune, not a heap of notes: your every act, dream, and slip plays the same "style of life," pointing to the same goal; change is hard because what must move is the whole tune.

⑥ There is only one yardstick for mental health — social interest: whether you can hold others in your heart, and are willing to cooperate and contribute to the community.

⑦ The drive for superiority is neutral; turned toward "contributing to others" the road grows warmer, turned toward "standing over others" it grows colder; the shared root of all failures is "caring only for oneself."

⑧ The exam of life has only three questions — work, fellowship, love — and not one can be solved alone; they all demand cooperation. The evader's favorite trick is "yes, but…," a dignified excuse that lifts him out of the hall.

⑨ Don't confuse the inferiority feeling with the inferiority complex: the first is the engine, the second is being crushed by it and using it as a permanent excuse; and the more ferociously one flaunts superiority, the deeper the inferiority usually hidden beneath.

⑩ The core of upbringing is not to satisfy the child but to teach the child to cooperate; and the meaning of life comes down to one word — contribution: adjusting the lamp in your heart that lights only yourself until it can also light up others.
