Crime and Punishment

Crime and Punishment · Fyodor Dostoevsky · 1866

中文 →

In one sentence

A destitute student talks himself into a theory: a few "extraordinary men," for the sake of a higher end, have the right to step over morality — even to kill. He actually does it — and then the book spends five hundred pages proving one thing: you can sign yourself a permit to murder in your head, but your conscience refuses to stamp it. What destroys him is not the law, not the police, not Siberia, but the discovery that the thing living inside him on behalf of his victims has made it impossible for him to go on living himself. This is not a murder mystery (the killer is fixed by chapter one). It is an experiment that pushes one question — can reason license a killing? — all the way to the edge.

Where it sits

Published in 1866, Crime and Punishment is Dostoevsky's first great novel after his return from Siberian exile — in his twenties he was sentenced to death for joining a radical circle, reprieved at the very moment of execution, and sent to hard labor. That experience of "having died once" turned a progressive young man into the deepest doubter of the idea that "reason can save mankind." He would drill into the same theme for the rest of his life, and Crime and Punishment is where that line first takes full shape: it precedes The Brothers Karamazov (this site's Read 23a) by fourteen years and reads as its rehearsal — the earlier book runs the idea that "everything is permitted" to completion inside one man, the later one inside a whole family and world. For the fuller panorama of Dostoevsky's thought, read 23a; this page just distills this one case.

The core claims

  • The crime isn't in the law; the punishment isn't in the prison. The "crime" is an idea — issuing reason's permit to kill; the "punishment" is conscience, which detonates before any trial and slowly cuts the murderer out of the human race. The real cell is inside him.
  • An idea can grow an axe. Raskolnikov kills not for money (he barely touches the loot) but to test a theory — and to test whether he is the kind of man who "has the right." What Dostoevsky proves is that an abstract idea, once taken seriously, really does turn into action, into blood.
  • It is a trial of an entire era. Through one murder Dostoevsky prosecutes the rationalism, utilitarianism, and nihilism fashionable in 1860s Russia: once reason breaks free of conscience and faith, the arithmetic of "kill one harmful louse for the happiness of the many" ends up charged, in full, to the killer himself.

The core concepts, one by one

The "Extraordinary Man" theory: humanity split into material and lawgivers

This is the engine of the book. In an article titled "On Crime," Raskolnikov argues that humanity divides into two kinds. The vast majority are "ordinary" — "material" for reproducing the species, born to obey and keep the law. A tiny few are "extraordinary" — the Newtons and Napoleons who move history and, in order to bring the world a new idea, have the right to cross every moral line, to march over corpses and shed blood with a clear conscience. In his view, every true lawgiver and teacher of mankind was, by definition, a "criminal" — each of them broke the old law.

The trick at the theory's heart is this: it swaps "is killing wrong?" for "are you entitled to kill?" Morality stops being a commandment over everyone's head and becomes a tiered threshold — the superman who clears it gets to legislate for himself. This precedes Nietzsche's "Übermensch" by more than a decade, yet Dostoevsky's verdict is exactly the reverse: where Nietzsche would have man cross beyond good and evil, Dostoevsky has his superman-candidate crushed alive by his own conscience. You can argue in your head that you have the right to kill; your conscience will sentence you to death on your victims' behalf.

"Am I a trembling creature, or do I have the right?" — murder as a self-test

Raskolnikov splits open the skull of Alyona, an old pawnbroker — a "useless, wicked louse" who in his eyes only sucks others' blood. But as he does it, her innocent, gentle, pregnant half-sister Lizaveta walks in, and he kills her too. This stroke is the book's cruelest irony: the instant the theory touches ground, the first blood it spills lands on exactly the kind of weak, harmless person it claimed to protect.

He keeps asking himself one line: "Am I a trembling creature, or do I have the right?" The murder is a proof he sets himself — proof that he belongs to the "extraordinary" class. But the moment blood flows, the answer arrives: he cannot step over it coolly like a Napoleon; he collapses into fever, delirium, nausea and terror. What the murder proves is precisely the conclusion he most dreaded — he is no Napoleon; he too is only a louse. The superman's freedom the theory promised him, he never gets to taste for even a second.

The utilitarian "arithmetic": one life for a thousand — a good deal?

Before the murder, Raskolnikov overhears a student in a tavern voicing his exact thought: this pawnbroker is venomous and greedy, driving so many to ruin; wouldn't it be a good deal to kill her and use her money to rescue hundreds of promising young lives? One harmful life for a thousand useful ones — by arithmetic, it's simply "pest control for the public good." This "tavern arithmetic" is the book's most direct quotation and mockery of utilitarianism and the radical "rational egoism" then in vogue (the doctrine, preached in Chernyshevsky's What Is to Be Done?, that if people would only calculate their own interest cleverly enough, society would automatically progress).

Dostoevsky's rebuttal isn't an argument; it's making you watch the sum actually get carried out: however elegant "one for a thousand" looks on paper, in reality it is one human being splitting open another's skull with an axe — plus an innocent Lizaveta. Numbers round off; human lives don't. No matter how correct the sum, it cannot grow one shred of a right to kill. That is Dostoevsky's verdict on the whole project of arranging good and evil by rational calculation.

Conscience as the sentence: his very name means "split"

The surname "Raskolnikov" comes from the Russian raskol (schism, split) — he is a man cleft in two: half cold theorist, half the soft-hearted young man who empties his pockets for a drunkard's family. After the murder that fissure tears wide open. What truly punishes him is never external — not the manhunt, not the interrogation, not the exile, but the utter isolation of having cut himself out of the human race. He meets his own mother and sister and finds he can no longer love them, no longer come near them like a normal person — between him and everyone else stands a wall of blood he built with his own hands.

This is one of Dostoevsky's most startling insights: the punishment happens before the trial, and it is heavier than any sentence. The law's "punishment" is imposed by society and wiped clean once you've served your term; conscience's "punishment" is one you cannot stop executing on yourself — it doesn't ask whether you were caught, only whether you shed that drop of blood. When Raskolnikov finally confesses, it is less because Porfiry cornered him than because he can no longer bear the weight of "living while already sentenced to death by himself."

Sonya and "suffering-into-redemption": calling a man out of the grave

If the theory shoves him into the abyss, the one pulling him back up is Sonya — a girl forced into prostitution to feed her drunkard father and her stepmother's children, and yet the purest, least calculating believer in the whole book. Dostoevsky deliberately places holiness in a "prostitute": true goodness lives not in the mind's theories but in a person like this, trodden into the mud by life and still refusing to give up love and faith. She and he — one cast out for crime, one for shame — are both ruled "out" by the world, and so they recognize each other.

The book's spiritual summit is Sonya reading Raskolnikov the passage from John's Gospel on the raising of Lazarus (Lazarus, the dead man Jesus calls back out of the tomb, a figure of death-into-resurrection) — not just a story, but a whole way out laid before him: you are spiritually dead, but even the dead can be called back to life, if you will turn around. Sonya's instruction is utterly concrete: go to the crossroads, kneel, kiss the earth you have defiled, and confess your crime aloud to everyone. Redemption is not grasping an idea; it is bending down again and again, owning it publicly, and accepting suffering (stradanie, which for Dostoevsky is the necessary road to purifying the soul and reconnecting with mankind). In the end she follows him to Siberia. What resurrects him is not logic but love.

Two dark mirrors: Svidrigailov and Luzhin

Dostoevsky loves to expose his protagonist through a "double (dvойnik)," and he gives Raskolnikov two mirrors that carry his logic to its end. Svidrigailov is what "a superman without a conscience" actually looks like — a man long past every moral line, acting on pure appetite, toying with others, suspected of darker crimes, his face a mask of nihilistic calm. He is the terminus of Raskolnikov's theory fully cashed out, and the terminus is boundless emptiness, ending in suicide. He says something chilling (paraphrased): "We always imagine eternity as something vast, but maybe it's just a little country bathhouse, sooty in the corners, with spiders in every one." Strip a man of conscience and meaning, and eternity shrinks to that one grimy room.

The other mirror is Luzhin — the suitor of Raskolnikov's sister, a polished utilitarian egoist who preaches the "economic man" creed that if everyone just looks after himself, society comes out optimal. He never kills anyone, yet uses decent-looking calculation to treat those around him as tools. Dostoevsky pincers his hero between the two: one (Svidrigailov) proves that "no conscience" leads to emptiness and self-destruction; the other (Luzhin) proves that "rational self-interest" is, at bottom, merely vile. Watching them, Raskolnikov is watching the two possible fates of his own theory.

Porfiry: a psychological hunt with no evidence

The examining magistrate Porfiry Petrovich is one of the most fascinating interrogators in literature. He holds not a shred of hard evidence, yet turns the questioning into a cat-and-mouse of the mind — he has read Raskolnikov's "On Crime" and sees straight through this proud young man, peeling back his defenses with what sounds like idle chatter. But the most telling thing is this: Porfiry finally urges him to confess, and the reason is not "you can't escape" but "go and suffer — suffering can make you a man again." This is exactly where Dostoevsky parts ways with modern justice: what he truly cares about is not convicting and jailing the killer, but whether this soul can turn back. The law wants a closed case; Dostoevsky wants a resurrection.

Petersburg: a city that drives you mad

Don't overlook the book's space. Dostoevsky's St. Petersburg is a summer of heat, yellow haze, stench, crush and squeeze — scaffolding, taverns, cramped "coffin-like" rented rooms, sour air, the whole city as suffocating as the hero's feverish head. This setting isn't backdrop; it's the theory's breeding ground: poverty presses a man step by step into a corner, and it's in the corner that he grabs at a dangerous straw like the "extraordinary man" theory. The drunkard Marmeladov says something that lands hard (paraphrased): "Do you understand what it means when a man has nowhere left to go?" It is exactly this "nowhere left to go" that moves a young man not evil by nature to the thought of murder.

The load-bearing skeleton

Here's the one thing this thick book keeps circling, said once and clearly: what it argues, and what it argues from.

Its claim is this: an idea can grow into an axe; and the axe, in the end, splits open the very hand that wields it. Raskolnikov first builds, in his head, the theory that "the extraordinary have the right to shed blood," and uses it to issue himself a license to kill; the axe falls, but the theory never delivers the superman's freedom it promised — instead it lets loose the one beast he cannot cage, conscience, which drives him all the way to kneeling and confessing at the crossroads.

What does it argue from? Not from the author stepping out to preach, but from running the theory, start to finish, inside a living man. Dostoevsky pushes atheism-rationalism-superman-will to the end of its logic and lets you see with your own eyes what stands there — blood, madness, a Svidrigailov-style suicide. And the one cure he offers, he gives not through argument but through a person, Sonya: not a cleverer arithmetic, but love, confession, the willingness to suffer, and bending back down to reconnect with the earth and with everyone. A sickness made in the head can only be healed by the heart.

Common misreadings & criticism

  • Don't read it as a detective story: the killer is revealed in chapter one; the suspense was never "who did it" but "will conscience sentence him, and how." Read as crime fiction, it is entirely missed.
  • The "extraordinary man" theory is not Dostoevsky's position — it's the target he means to demolish: he writes it so eloquently and seductively not because he agrees, but because he wants it to live at full strength before detonating in reality. A reader who takes Raskolnikov's manifesto for the author's own voice has been fooled into the opposite.
  • Is the epilogue's conversion too abrupt? (the most famous criticism): many (Nabokov among them) find that the hero's sudden illumination by love and faith in Siberia comes too fast and too sentimentally, out of key with the cold psychological realism of the preceding five hundred pages — as if the author pressed a bright ending on by force. The defense: Dostoevsky says outright at the close that this is only "the beginning of a new story," the start of resurrection, not its completion — the true rebirth left to a sequel he never lived to write.
  • Its prophetic force: the logic that "a few, for a higher end, have the right to cross morality and sacrifice the few" was cashed out by twentieth-century totalitarianisms into blood on a mass scale — seen this way, Crime and Punishment is the earliest and most accurate warning against "killing in the name of an ideal."
  • Polyphony: the critic Bakhtin drew from Dostoevsky's novels the idea of the "polyphonic novel" — meaning that the characters' ideas are not the author's mouthpieces but independent, equal, mutually arguing "voices." That is why Raskolnikov's theory, Sonya's faith, and Svidrigailov's nihilism are each written to full strength, colliding without the author striking a final gavel. This is not muddled conviction but a deliberate choice to let every idea live as a person, then set them arguing with live ammunition.

The distillation in ten sentences

① This is no whodunit: the killer is fixed in chapter one; the real suspense is whether, and how, a man's conscience will sentence him after the murder.

② The "extraordinary man" theory: humanity splits in two — the ordinary many fit only to obey, a few "Napoleonic" supermen entitled, for a higher end, to cross morality and even shed blood. It swaps "is killing wrong?" for "are you entitled to kill?"

③ This superman logic precedes Nietzsche by over a decade, and Dostoevsky's verdict is the reverse: you can argue you have the right to kill; your conscience will sentence you to death on your victims' behalf.

④ He kills not for money but as a self-test — "Am I a trembling creature, or do I have the right?" The moment blood flows, the answer comes: he is no Napoleon; he too is only a louse.

⑤ The instant the theory touches ground, the first blood spills on the weak it claimed to protect: he kills the innocent Lizaveta too.

⑥ The "tavern arithmetic" — kill one harmful old woman, save hundreds with her money, a "good deal" on paper. Dostoevsky's rebuttal is to carry the sum out for you: numbers round off, lives don't, and no correct sum grows a right to kill.

⑦ Punishment comes before the trial and weighs more than any sentence: the law's is wiped clean once served, conscience's you cannot stop executing on yourself — his surname, raskol, means "split."

⑧ What calls him out of the grave is not logic but love: Sonya, selling herself to feed her family yet purest in faith, reads him "the raising of Lazarus" and sends him to the crossroads to kneel, kiss the earth, and confess aloud.

⑨ Two dark mirrors show the theory's fate: Svidrigailov (the conscienceless superman → emptiness and suicide, "eternity may be just a bathhouse full of spiders") and Luzhin (the "economic man" of rational self-interest → vileness at the core).

⑩ The whole book in one line: an idea can grow into an axe, and the axe splits the hand that holds it; a sickness made in the head is healed only by the heart — not a cleverer arithmetic, but love, confession, and the willingness to suffer.
