Bhagavad Gita

The Song of the Lord · embedded in the epic Mahabharata · attributed to Vyasa · c. 2nd century BCE – 2nd century CE

中文 →

In One Sentence

You cannot escape action — to be alive is to keep doing, and even "doing nothing" is a kind of doing. So the problem the Gita sets out to solve is: if a human being can never opt out of action, how do you act without being enslaved by the results, without accruing karmic debt, without losing your inner freedom? Its answer is as heavy as a single line: do what is yours to do, but let go of the fruit — you have a right to the action, never to its results. To act without grasping is to be free.

Coordinates

The Bhagavad Gita ("Song of the Lord") is not a standalone book but a 700-verse passage inside India's great epic, the Mahabharata. Tradition ascribes it to the legendary compiler Vyasa; in fact it is almost certainly a layered composite, assembled over centuries around the turn of the common era, weaving together Samkhya, Yoga, Vedanta and the rising devotional current of bhakti. It is Hinduism's most widely read and most heavily commented-upon scripture — Shankara and Ramanuja both wrote commentaries on it, and Gandhi carried it with him all his life. On the map of Eastern wisdom it is the "third pole," standing alongside Daoism and Buddhism: where Daoism teaches non-doing and Buddhism teaches no-self, the Gita offers a third road — liberation found within action, in the thick of the world. You need not withdraw, need not go limp; you can act fully and still not be dragged off by the world.

Central Claims

The whole text is a single battlefield conversation, circling three things:

  • Fleeing action is no way out; transcending your attachment to results is. You cannot buy peace by doing nothing — life is condemned to move. What you can change is why you act and with what mind.
  • Do your own duty (svadharma), and treat action as an offering rather than a transaction. Not for the payoff, but because this is the post you are meant to hold.
  • The final move is to surrender the whole self to God (bhakti, loving devotion). Knowledge, action and devotion converge into one road: only when the small self stops grasping does a person truly come to rest.

The Core Concepts, One by One

The Battlefield as Predicament: A Man Breaks Down at the Moment He Must Act

The stage for the whole book is a civil war about to begin. Two armies face off on the field of Kurukshetra. The prince Arjuna sits in his chariot and asks his charioteer — who is in fact the great god Krishna in human form — to drive between the ranks. He looks up and sees, on the opposing side, his own cousins, teachers and elders. At the thought of killing his kin and mentors for the sake of a throne, he collapses: his limbs go slack, the bow slips from his hand, he says "I would rather be killed than strike," and sinks down. This is chapter one, "The Despair of Arjuna."

Why would a scripture open with a man falling apart? Because what it is out to handle is precisely the moral dilemma itself: when two rights tear against each other — it is right not to kill your family, and it is right to do your duty — what is a person to do? Many read the whole battlefield as allegory: Kurukshetra is the human heart, and the great war is the daily battle inside you between good and evil, duty and private feeling. Arjuna's paralysis is the archetype of everyone who thinks so much they cannot move. Krishna's teaching over the next seventeen chapters is the work of carrying him, step by step, from "I cannot act" to "how, then, shall I act."

The Deathless Self (Atman): What Perishes Is the Body, Not You

Krishna's first hammer-blow is to redefine death. He says: the real you — the Self (Atman) — is never born and never dies; weapons cannot cut it, fire cannot burn it, water cannot wet it, wind cannot dry it. What dies is only the body, a garment. There is a famous simile: "As a person casts off worn-out clothes and puts on new ones, so the embodied Self casts off worn-out bodies and takes on new." (paraphrased, 2.22)

This is not a justification for killing (a point the "Criticisms" section will meet honestly), but a way to loosen the premise Arjuna is clutching so hard — that death is the ultimate evil. In the Gita's world, life turns through endless rebirth (samsara); bodies come and go, while the awareness that watches it all never stirs. It changes the way you see things like this: once you stop treating "the gain and loss and survival of the flesh" as the whole of the matter, many of the fears that paralyze you drop a notch. The real question is no longer "will I lose," but "with what mind should I act right now." This step is the foundation for what follows — you must first believe there is a "you" that does not rise and fall with fortune before non-attachment becomes even thinkable.

The Yoga of Action (karma yoga): You Have a Right to the Action, Not to the Fruit

This is the crown of the book, and the most famous line it ever gave the world. Arjuna thinks, "Then why not just do nothing — go off to the mountains and find peace?" Krishna answers: impossible — no one can stay actionless even for a moment; merely keeping this body alive, eating, breathing, is action, and "non-action" is itself a kind of action. Fleeing to a cave does not let you opt out of cause and effect; you have only changed posture and kept moving.

Since motion is inescapable, the way out lies not in "whether to act" but in "how." Hence the great verse 2.47: "You have a right to the action alone, never to its fruits. Let not the results be your motive, but do not let that pull you into inaction either." (paraphrased) Unpacked, it is four things: ① do what must be done; ② put your attention on doing the thing well and rightly, not on what it will bring you; ③ do not act for the payoff — whether and how much reward arrives is decided by countless conditions outside your hands; ④ yet "not for the payoff" does not mean slacking — on the contrary, it means throwing yourself into the action itself more purely and with more focus.

A modern image: a real craftsman making a table of course wants the money, but as he planes the board what fills his mind is "this edge must run true, this joint must sit tight," not "how much will this sell for" — the moment that second thought arises, his hand wavers. Karma yoga is exactly this: pull your mind back off the result and stake it all on the action, and the work comes out better while you yourself grow steadier. It hands Arjuna — and every modern person crushed by outcome-anxiety — a way through: you need not flee the world; the very thing you are doing can be your practice.

Acting Without Grasping (nishkama karma): Let the Result Go, and the Mind Is Free

The heart of karma yoga is one term: nishkama karma (literally "desireless action") — carrying out the deed while not clutching the result. Krishna gives it a definition: "Yoga is evenness of mind (samatva)." (2.48, paraphrased) Success and failure look the same, gain and loss the same, blame and praise the same — not numbness, but an inner steadiness that the ups and downs cannot drag around. He has a crisper version too: "Yoga is skill in action" (yogah karmasu kaushalam, 2.50, paraphrased) — the true master is not one who avoids doing, but one whose mind stays unruffled while doing.

At the close of chapter two he sketches the ideal figure: the sthitaprajna, the one settled in wisdom. He "draws his senses back from their objects as a tortoise draws in its limbs"; he is "like the ocean — into which all rivers pour, day and night, yet whose level does not rise and whose surface is not churned." Desire comes in, and he is not swept away; pain arrives, and he does not panic. Here is the key mechanism: what makes us suffer is often not the thing itself but our death-grip on the result — "I must win," "I must not lose." The moment that hand relaxes, the situation is the same, but the mind is free.

How does it change the way you see the world? It pries apart two things we habitually confuse — effort and anxiety. You can give everything and still not be anxious, because you are responsible only for what you can control (the action, the attitude, the commitment) and you hand over what you cannot (results, other people's reactions, luck). This runs strikingly parallel to the Stoics' "dichotomy of control," and it is one of the oldest antidotes we have for the modern disease of outcome-anxiety.

Your Own Duty (svadharma): Do What Is Yours, Even Clumsily

Arjuna is a kshatriya, of the warrior order, and to fight in defense of the realm is his svadharma (literally "one's own law/duty"). Krishna says something to him of great weight: "Better to do your own duty poorly than another's duty well." (paraphrased, 3.35 / 18.47) The meaning: a person should hold the place and responsibility that is genuinely theirs, rather than envy and imitate someone else's way of living; even if you do your own job roughly, that beats abandoning it to go play a role that is not yours.

Why does it matter? Because it supplies the "what to do" half that non-attachment left open. Karma yoga settles "with what mind to act"; svadharma settles "which act" — and the answer is: do the one that has landed on your shoulders, not the loftier, more glamorous one you imagine. For a modern reader this is a splash of cold water: instead of forever fretting "did I pick the wrong track, is someone else's work better," shoulder the responsibility already in front of you, solidly. (Of course, chaining svadharma to hereditary caste is exactly where the book draws its heaviest fire — more on that below.)

The Three Gunas: Everything Is Braided from Three Strands

The Gita borrows the Samkhya framework to explain "why a person is the way they are." All of nature (prakriti, primal matter) is braided from three basic "strands" — the three gunas (literally "cords") — and every person and every thing is a product of these three in some proportion:

  • Sattva (goodness / light): clarity, harmony, calm, upliftment — the mind at its most translucent. But it too "binds you, through happiness and knowledge": you become attached to feeling "I am so serene, I am so wise."
  • Rajas (passion / restlessness): desire, activity, ambition, unease — the force that pushes you to chase, possess, never stop. It "binds through attachment to the fruits of action."
  • Tamas (darkness / inertia): dullness, sloth, delusion, procrastination — the force that pins you where you are. It "binds through heedlessness and torpor."

The subtle point: even "good" sattva is a cord. However you rebalance the three, at bottom they all bind; only the pattern of the rope differs. So the Gita's goal is not "stockpile more sattva" but to go beyond the gunas (gunatita) — to recognize that "three strands rise and fall within me, but the 'I' that watches them rise and fall is not among them." Here is how it changes the way you see the world: when you notice that your temper, your craving, your laziness are just "rajas stirring, tamas pressing down," rather than "this is simply how rotten I am," a gap opens between you and your emotions — you are no longer the emotion, you are the one watching it.

Three Roads Made One, with Devotion (bhakti) as Home

The Gita's genius is that it does not force a binary choice; it gathers up several roads to liberation that Indian tradition had kept separate: the road of knowledge (jnana yoga, liberation through insight into "who I am"), the road of action (karma yoga, liberation through unattached action), and the road of meditation (dhyana yoga, liberation by gathering the mind into stillness). It says these roads meet at the same summit; walk whichever suits your temperament.

But as the text goes on, one road — the most accessible of all — rises to the fore: the road of devotion (bhakti yoga, love of God). Krishna says: "Whoever offers me, with love, a leaf, a flower, a fruit, a little water — that I accept." (paraphrased, 9.26) Liberation, in other words, need not rest on high philosophical subtlety or brutal asceticism; a single heart offered in sincerity is enough — and with that, the highest wisdom is thrown open to every ordinary person. The book's last great stroke is the verse countless readers take as its whole summary, 18.66: "Abandon all dharmas, all the burdens of duty, and simply take refuge in me; I shall free you from every sin — do not grieve." (paraphrased)

This is where the book closes the circle: acting without grasping needs, in the end, an answer to "hand the result to whom?" — and the answer is: to God. You do your duty, you carry out your action, and you entrust success and failure to a whole larger than yourself. Here is how it changes the way you see the world: when action is no longer "I alone bear the entire result" but "I do what is mine and hand over the rest," the crushing weight of fighting solo, of anxious clinging, is more than half lifted.

The Cosmic Form: "I Am Time, the Devourer of Worlds"

Chapter eleven is the emotional peak of the whole book. Arjuna asks to see Krishna's true form, and Krishna grants him a "divine eye" — in an instant Arjuna beholds the Vishvarupa, the universal form: countless faces, countless arms, endless suns and moons, all beings and all worlds arising and perishing within this one body, blazing with light, without beginning or end. He sees the warriors of both armies rush into that vast mouth and be ground to dust. Terrified out of his wits, begging for mercy, he asks, "Who, then, are you?" Krishna answers with the line that would shake the ages: "I am Time (kala), the great devourer of worlds, and I have come forth to destroy these people." (paraphrased, 11.32)

The weight of that line was struck again in the twentieth century: watching the first nuclear test, Robert Oppenheimer, the father of the atomic bomb, found this very verse rising in his mind — "Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds." What the chapter tells Arjuna is this: the death of these men is already decreed in the great wheel of cosmic time; you are only the instrument (nimitta, "occasion / tool") this vast hand has borrowed for a moment. You imagine "it is I who kill," but birth and death run on a larger process, and all you can do is fulfill your duty and be a good "tool." This is at once a consolation to Arjuna and the book's most unsettling passage — it sets a person's moral responsibility inside a cosmic vision so immense it verges on the cold.

The Essential Skeleton

The eighteen chapters trace one clear line, from "paralysis" to "action":

  • Collapse (ch. 1): a man, at the moment he must act, is broken by a moral dilemma; the bow falls from his hand.
  • Loosening the ground (ch. 2): first establish the deathless Self, then fire off the book's master-thesis, "you have a right to the action, not to the fruit," and sketch the "one settled in wisdom."
  • Breaking the illusion that "peace lies in doing nothing" (ch. 3–6): prove that a human cannot not-act; the way out is unattached action (karma yoga) and holding one's own duty (svadharma), then knowledge and meditation.
  • Bringing in God and devotion (ch. 7–12): descend from the formless Brahman to a personal God one can love and entrust oneself to; bhakti becomes the most universal road, with the cosmic form of chapter 11 as the emotional peak.
  • Drawing in the net (ch. 13–18): gather the whole framework with the gunas, and "the field and the knower of the field" (body and Self), arriving at 18.66, "let go of all, take refuge in me." Arjuna rises: "My doubt is gone; I will do as you say."

What, in the end, does it establish? — Engagement and renunciation need not be an either/or. You do not have to throw off your responsibilities and flee to the forest to win liberation; drop the small self's grasping after results, and the action right in front of you becomes the practice. This is the Gita's core contribution: reconciling India's two great ideals, the active engaged life (pravritti) and the withdrawn renunciant life (nivritti).

Common Misreadings & Criticisms

  • Is it endorsing war and killing? (the one to face most squarely): the Gita is, after all, Krishna urging Arjuna into battle to kill. History holds two sharply opposed readings: Gandhi took it as pure allegory — Kurukshetra is the human heart, the war is the inner struggle of good and evil — and out of it he read the very doctrine of non-violence; while traditional and certain nationalist readings took it as a straightforward affirmation of "do your warrior's duty, and fight when you must." That tension is real; do not use one reading to erase the other.
  • Doesn't "non-attachment" become a license to do evil?: if "not attached to the result" means "whatever I do is fine so long as I stay detached inside," doesn't that open a back door to cold-blooded action? This is the most dangerous misreading of karma yoga. The text's constraint is that non-attachment must be bound to svadharma (legitimate duty) and to devotion (offering the act to God) — it is not "do whatever you like," but "do what is rightfully yours, and not for private desire." Cut loose from those two, "non-attachment" is hollowed out.
  • The defense of caste: verse 4.13 says "the four varnas were created by me according to guna and karma," and svadharma is often read as a sanctification of the hereditary caste order. The Dalit leader B. R. Ambedkar attacked the Gita fiercely as a brief for an unequal caste system and for the doctrine of "kill when killing is called for." To read it today, one must separate the wisdom of "hold your own duty" from the rot of "your duty is fixed by the birth you were assigned" — keep the first, discard the second.
  • It is in fact a layered composite: the Gita reads like one seamless discourse, but scholars broadly agree it stitches together several originally distinct systems — Samkhya, Yoga, Vedanta and bhakti — over a span of centuries. So it is not internally seamless: between "the formless Brahman" and "the lovable personal god Krishna," between "knowledge is supreme" and "devotion is supreme," there are seams never fully closed. To take it as a single author's philosophy composed in one breath is a beautiful misunderstanding.
  • Half of its "supreme scripture" status is a modern making: important though the Gita is within Hinduism, its elevation into "the Hindu Bible" and its passage to the wider world were largely accomplished after the first English translation (1785) — through the Theosophical Society, through Vivekananda, and above all through Gandhi's modern championing. This takes nothing from its value, but it is a reminder: the "Gita" we read today comes wearing a modern lens as well.

The Essence in Ten Lines

① You cannot escape action — to be alive is to keep doing, and even "doing nothing" is a kind of doing; so the question is never "act or not," but "with what mind."

② The master-thesis is one line: you have a right to the action, never to its fruit — give your all, but do not let "chasing the result" be your motive, and do not let that pull you into slacking.

③ Nishkama karma: relax the hand that clutches the result, and the situation is unchanged but the mind is free — what makes us suffer is usually not the event but the fixation that "I must win."

④ "Yoga is evenness of mind": success and failure, praise and blame, gain and loss all look the same — not numbness, but an inner steadiness the ups and downs cannot drag around; the true master's mind stays unruffled while acting.

⑤ What perishes is only the body, a worn garment; the Self (Atman) cannot be cut by weapons or burned by fire — you must first trust there is a "you" that does not rise and fall with fortune before non-attachment is even possible.

⑥ Better to do your own duty clumsily than to play another's role brilliantly — rather than fret "is someone else's track better," first shoulder the one thing that has landed on you.

⑦ Your temper, craving and sloth are just the three gunas — goodness, passion, inertia — waxing and waning in you; see that, and you no longer "are" the emotion but "watch" it; even "good" sattva is a cord.

⑧ Knowledge, action and devotion meet at one summit; and the most accessible road is devotion: "offer me a leaf, a flower, a fruit, a little water in love, and I accept it" — the highest wisdom thrown open to every ordinary person.

⑨ "I am Time, the devourer of worlds" — birth and death run on a process vaster than you, and you are only the instrument it has borrowed for a moment; do your duty, and hand over the rest.

⑩ The book carries Arjuna from "I cannot act" to "how shall I act," and establishes one thing: engagement and renunciation need not be an either/or; drop the small self's grasp on results, and the action itself becomes the practice — you need not flee the world to be free.
